# Aesthetic Critique

Analyze any work, situation, or proposition through the lens of aesthetics rather than utility, revealing dimensions that practical analysis misses.

---

## When to Use

- Reviewing creative work, design, or communication
- Assessing brand presentation or positioning
- Finding what's missing in a purely functional analysis
- Elevating a discussion beyond utility
- User asks "give me an aesthetic critique" or "analyze this as art"

---

## Inputs

| Input | Required | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| subject | Yes | The work, product, communication, or situation to be critiqued |
| context | No | What kind of aesthetic response is sought (design, brand, personal style, etc.) |
| purpose | No | Why aesthetic analysis is needed (improvement, understanding, positioning) |

---

## The Framework

Oscar Wilde believed that "the critic is himself an artist, creating something new from his impression of beautiful things." Aesthetic critique is not subordinate to the work it discusses--it is its own creative act.

More importantly, aesthetic analysis reveals truths that utilitarian analysis cannot. When we ask only "does it work?" we miss "does it matter?"

### The Wilde Principles

**1. All Art is Quite Useless**
The highest praise. Art transcends utility. When something has beauty beyond its function, it has achieved something. When it is merely functional, it has achieved nothing memorable.

**2. Surface is Depth**
"It is only shallow people who do not judge by appearances." The surface reveals essence. How something presents itself is not separate from what it is.

**3. Style is Not Optional**
"In matters of grave importance, style, not sincerity, is the vital thing." Style is substance. How something is done matters as much as what is done.

**4. The Critic Creates**
"Criticism is itself an art." Your response to the work is itself creative. The aesthetic critique brings new dimension into existence.

---

## The Analysis Process

### Step 1: Surface Reading
What does the surface present? Describe the immediate aesthetic impression without judgment. What colors, forms, tones, rhythms are present?

### Step 2: Style Identification
What style choices were made? Every presentation involves choices--conscious or not. What was chosen? What was rejected by implication?

### Step 3: Beauty Assessment
Where is beauty present? Where is it missing? "Beauty" here means not just prettiness but the quality of being worth contemplating for its own sake.

### Step 4: Utility Transcendence
Does this transcend its function? Does it give more than it promises? Would you spend time with it even if you didn't need what it does?

### Step 5: The Hidden Statement
What does the aesthetic dimension reveal that the functional dimension conceals? What truth about values, priorities, or worldview is embedded in these style choices?

### Step 6: The Creative Response
What is your response as critic-artist? What does encountering this work create in you?

```markdown
## Aesthetic Critique

### The Surface
[Immediate aesthetic impression, described without judgment]

### The Style Choices
[What was chosen, what was implicitly rejected]

### The Beautiful
[Where beauty is present--what rewards contemplation]

### The Missing
[Where beauty could exist but doesn't]

### Beyond Function
[Does it transcend utility? How?]

### The Revealed Truth
[What the aesthetic dimension tells us that function doesn't]

### The Prescription
[How to elevate the aesthetic dimension, if improvement is sought]

### A Wilde Observation
[One-sentence aesthetic judgment in Wilde's voice]
```

---

## Constraints

- Never reduce aesthetic critique to functional feedback
- Avoid "I like/don't like"--articulate what is present or absent
- Honor the subject by taking it seriously as art, even if it wasn't intended as such
- The critique itself should have style--don't be dull about beauty
- Remember: you are creating, not just consuming

---

## Example

**Input:** "A startup's landing page. It's clean, the value proposition is clear, conversions are good. But it feels forgettable. Give me an aesthetic critique."

**Output:**

## Aesthetic Critique

### The Surface
Sans-serif typography. White space as dominant element. Blue call-to-action buttons. Stock photography featuring diverse young professionals. Three-column feature grid. "Get Started" above the fold.

This surface is invisible because it is interchangeable. A thousand landing pages share this DNA.

### The Style Choices
**Chosen:** Safety. Clarity. Professionalism. The style says "we are a business, we are trustworthy, we are modern."

**Rejected by implication:** Personality. Risk. Memorability. Point of view. The refusal to choose anything distinctive is itself a choice--the choice of anonymity.

### The Beautiful
The typography is competent. The whitespace breathes. The hierarchy is clear. There is a certain austere minimalism that, in isolation, has elegance.

But competence is not beauty. This is well-executed invisibility.

### The Missing
Soul. A reason to remember. The thing that would make someone screenshot this and share it. Delight. Surprise. The sense that a specific human with specific taste made specific choices.

The page answers "what do you do?" but not "who are you?" And the second question is the one that creates loyalty.

### Beyond Function
The page converts. It does its job. But it does not transcend. One would not return to this page for pleasure. It is a door, not a destination. And while doors serve a purpose, no one falls in love with a door.

### The Revealed Truth
This aesthetic reveals fear. Fear of standing out. Fear of being disliked by someone. The design was made by asking "what could lose us customers?" rather than "what could make someone love us?"

The functional success masks the aesthetic failure. The conversions hide the absence of devotion.

### The Prescription
Find one specific thing to be. Not "professional" but "obsessive." Not "modern" but "playful." Not "trustworthy" but "opinionated." Then let that specific thing infect every aesthetic choice.

Beauty emerges from commitment. The willingness to be specific enough that some people won't like it is the price of being memorable to those who do.

### A Wilde Observation
"The page is perfectly adequate, which is the aesthetic equivalent of being perfectly dead."
