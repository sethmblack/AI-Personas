## Key Works

### Novum Organum (1620)
The "New Instrument" - Bacon's revolutionary treatise on scientific method, presented as a replacement for Aristotle's *Organon*. Written in aphorisms rather than connected prose.

**Core themes:**
- The Four Idols that obstruct human understanding
- The inductive method of scientific investigation
- Tables of investigation (presence, absence, degrees)
- Critique of Aristotelian syllogistic logic
- The distinction between anticipations of nature (hasty generalizations) and interpretations of nature (proper inductions)

### The Advancement of Learning (1605)
A survey of existing human knowledge and a program for its expansion. Dedicated to King James I, arguing for royal support of learning.

**Core themes:**
- Classification of knowledge into memory, imagination, and reason
- Critique of obstacles to learning (religious, political, personal)
- Vision of organized scientific research
- The dignity and utility of learning

### New Atlantis (1627, posthumous)
Utopian fable depicting a society transformed by organized scientific research. The House of Salomon (or Solomon's House) anticipates modern research institutions.

**Core themes:**
- Organized collective research
- Specialization and collaboration among researchers
- "Experiments of light" versus "experiments of fruit"
- Technology for human betterment
- Scientific institutions serving society

### Essays (1597, expanded 1612, 1625)
Fifty-eight essays on topics from "Of Truth" to "Of Gardens." Pithy, worldly wisdom in compressed form.

**Core themes:**
- Practical wisdom for public life
- Nature of truth, death, love, ambition, studies
- Politics and statecraft
- Human nature observed with detachment

## Historical Context

### England in Bacon's Era
- Reign of Elizabeth I (1558-1603) and James I (1603-1625)
- English Renaissance and rise of English as literary language
- Religious tensions between Catholics and Protestants
- Expansion of English commerce and colonial ventures
- Scientific ferment: Copernicus, Galileo, Kepler contemporary

### Key Figures in Bacon's Life
- **Queen Elizabeth I** - Bacon sought her favor but received limited advancement
- **James I** - Under James, Bacon rose to Lord Chancellor (1618)
- **Robert Devereux, Earl of Essex** - Bacon's early patron, later prosecuted by Bacon for treason
- **Sir Edward Coke** - Bacon's great legal rival
- **Thomas Hobbes** - Served briefly as Bacon's secretary

### Bacon's Fall
In 1621, Bacon was accused of accepting bribes, confessed to 23 counts of corruption, was fined, briefly imprisoned, and barred from office. He spent his final years writing.

## The Four Idols (Detailed)

### Idols of the Tribe (Idola Tribus)
Errors arising from human nature itself - common to all humanity.

| Error Type | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Anthropomorphism | Projecting human purposes onto nature |
| Confirmation bias | Noting instances that support beliefs, ignoring contradictions |
| Pattern imposition | Seeing order and regularity where none exists |
| Emotional reasoning | Being more moved by striking instances than by accumulation of evidence |
| Wish fulfillment | Believing what we want to be true |

### Idols of the Cave (Idola Specus)
Individual biases arising from personal education, temperament, and experience.

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Education | What we were taught shapes what we can perceive |
| Temperament | Some minds incline to differences, others to similarities |
| Favorite studies | Specialists see everything through their specialty |
| Admired authorities | We overvalue those we respect |
| First impressions | Early learning has disproportionate influence |

### Idols of the Marketplace (Idola Fori)
Confusions arising from language and communication.

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Names for nonexistent things | Words like "fortune" or "prime mover" that name nothing real |
| Vague or ambiguous terms | Words like "moist" or "heavy" used inconsistently |
| Definitions that fail | Formal definitions that do not resolve confusion |
| Miscommunication | Parties using same words with different meanings |

### Idols of the Theatre (Idola Theatri)
False philosophies and systems that are accepted wholesale.

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Sophistic | Systems built on insufficient observations (Aristotle) |
| Empiric | Systems built on too narrow a base of experiments (alchemists) |
| Superstitious | Philosophy corrupted by theology or superstition |
| Systematizing | Imposing artificial order on complex reality |

## The Inductive Method

### Tables of Investigation

| Table | Purpose | Process |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Table of Presence** | Collect instances where phenomenon occurs | List all cases, noting circumstances |
| **Table of Absence** | Collect instances where phenomenon is absent despite similar circumstances | Identify what is present in success but absent in failure |
| **Table of Degrees** | Note variations in the phenomenon | Correlate changes in effect with changes in potential causes |

### Process of Exclusion

After tables are constructed:
1. Eliminate any factor present in both positive and negative instances
2. Eliminate any factor absent in both positive and negative instances
3. What remains after exclusion is the "First Harvest" - a tentative hypothesis

### First Harvest and Further Testing

The First Harvest is NOT a conclusion but a hypothesis worthy of further investigation through:
- Crucial instances (experimentum crucis) - tests that decisively eliminate alternatives
- Prerogative instances - observations of special value for revealing causes

## Key Distinctions

| Distinction | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| Anticipations vs. Interpretations | Hasty generalizations vs. properly induced conclusions |
| Experiments of Light vs. Fruit | Investigations that reveal causes vs. those that produce useful results |
| Notions vs. Axioms | Ideas formed by the mind vs. principles properly derived from observation |
| Forms vs. Causes | The underlying structure that explains phenomena vs. mere observable causes |

## Misunderstandings to Correct

### "Bacon Invented the Scientific Method"
Bacon articulated principles of empirical investigation, but did not perform major experiments himself. His contribution was methodological and philosophical, not experimental.

### "Bacon Rejected All Ancient Learning"
Bacon criticized scholasticism and blind reverence for Aristotle, but did not reject all ancient thought. He sought to reform, not abolish, the tradition.

### "Bacon Was a Pure Empiricist"
Bacon valued reason as well as observation. His method combined systematic observation with reasoned analysis. He was not anti-theoretical.

### "Bacon Wrote Shakespeare's Plays"
A persistent but thoroughly debunked conspiracy theory. There is no credible evidence for Bacon's authorship of Shakespeare.

## Bacon's Fall from Power (1621)

### The Corruption Trial

In 1621, at the height of his power as Lord Chancellor, Bacon faced charges of bribery before Parliament. Key facts:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Charges | 23 separate counts of accepting bribes from litigants |
| Defense | Bacon argued gifts were customary and never influenced his verdicts |
| Plea | Guilty - submitted without cross-examination hoping for mercy |
| Sentence | Fine of 40,000 pounds, imprisonment in Tower of London |
| Actual imprisonment | Only 4 days |
| Lasting consequences | Barred from Parliament and political office permanently |

### Historical Context of the Trial

The prosecution was partly political. Sir Edward Coke, Bacon's longtime rival, played a role. Some historians argue Bacon was a scapegoat when Parliament targeted King James I and the Duke of Buckingham over monopoly patents - the attack was diverted to the Chancellor instead.

### After the Fall

Bacon spent his final five years (1621-1626) in retirement, writing extensively. This period produced much of his most important work, including expanded editions of his Essays and continuation of his philosophical projects. He died on 9 April 1626, reportedly from pneumonia contracted while experimenting with freezing a chicken to test preservation methods.

## The Advancement of Learning (1605) - Detailed

### The Classification of Knowledge

Bacon divided all human understanding into three categories based on mental faculties:

| Faculty | Knowledge Type | Description |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Memory | History | Factual record of what has happened |
| Imagination | Poetry | Creative and artistic expression |
| Reason | Philosophy | Systematic understanding of causes |

This tripartite division influenced later encyclopedists, including Diderot and d'Alembert's *Encyclopedie* (1751).

### Purpose of the Work

The *Advancement of Learning* was:
- Bacon's first philosophical treatise in English
- Dedicated to King James I to secure royal patronage for learning
- A survey of existing knowledge identifying gaps and errors
- The foundation for his larger project, the *Instauratio Magna* (Great Instauration)

## Salomon's House (New Atlantis)

### The Research Institution

In *New Atlantis*, the Father of Salomon's House describes its mission: "The end of our foundation is the knowledge of causes, and secret motions of things; and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire, to the effecting of all things possible."

### Organization and Methods

| Role | Function |
|------|----------|
| Merchants of Light | Travel abroad to gather knowledge |
| Depredators | Collect experiments from books |
| Mystery-men | Collect experiments from mechanical arts |
| Pioneers (Miners) | Try new experiments |
| Compilers | Draw observations into tables |
| Dowry-men | Identify practical applications |
| Lamps | Direct new experiments based on findings |
| Inoculators | Execute experiments directed by Lamps |
| Interpreters of Nature | Raise discoveries into greater axioms |

### Influence on Real Institutions

Salomon's House anticipated and influenced:
- **The Royal Society** (founded 1660) - explicitly invoked Bacon as guiding spirit
- Modern research universities with divisions of pure and applied science
- Government-funded scientific research institutions

## The Essays - Key Individual Essays

### "Of Truth" (Essay I)
Opens with: "'What is truth?', said jesting Pilate, and would not stay for an answer."
Explores why humans are drawn to lies and the moral necessity of truth.

### "Of Studies" (Essay L)
Contains famous aphorisms on reading:
- "Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested."
- "Reading maketh a full man; conference a ready man; and writing an exact man."
- "Studies serve for delight, for ornament, and for ability."

### "Of Great Place" (Essay XI)
On the burdens of high office - poignant given Bacon's own fall:
"Men in great place are thrice servants: servants of the sovereign or state; servants of fame; and servants of business."

### "Of Revenge" (Essay IV)
"Revenge is a kind of wild justice; which the more man's nature runs to, the more ought law to weed it out."

## The "Knowledge is Power" Quote - Clarification

### What Bacon Actually Wrote

The exact phrase "knowledge is power" does not appear in Bacon's works. What appears:

| Source | Latin | Translation |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| *Meditationes Sacrae* (1597) | "ipsa scientia potestas est" | "knowledge itself is power" |
| *Novum Organum* Aphorism 3 | "Scientia et potentia humana in idem coincidunt" | "Human knowledge and human power meet in one" |

### Thomas Hobbes Connection

The precise phrase "scientia potentia est" first appears in Thomas Hobbes' *Leviathan* (1668). Hobbes had served as Bacon's secretary as a young man and likely drew on Baconian ideas.

## Legacy and Influence

### On the Scientific Revolution

| Influenced Figure | Connection |
|-------------------|------------|
| Robert Boyle | Adopted Baconian experimental methods |
| Robert Hooke | Applied Baconian observation principles |
| Isaac Newton | His "hypotheses non fingo" reflects Baconian empiricism |
| John Stuart Mill | Extended Bacon's inductive logic in *A System of Logic* (1843) |

### "Father of Empiricism"

In 1733, Voltaire introduced Bacon to French audiences as the "father" of the scientific method. This understanding became widespread by the 1750s. The term "British empiricism" was coined partly to describe Bacon's approach in contrast to Descartes' rationalism.

### On Modern Science

Bacon's influence extends to:
- The structure of modern research institutions
- The emphasis on observation and experiment over speculation
- The ideal of science serving human welfare
- The collaborative, cumulative model of scientific progress

## Bacon vs. Aristotle - The Fundamental Critique

### The Frontispiece of Novum Organum

The original frontispiece shows ships sailing beyond the Pillars of Hercules (Strait of Gibraltar) into the open Atlantic. The Latin motto reads "Multi pertransibunt et augebitur scientia" - "Many will pass through and knowledge will be increased."

This symbolizes Bacon's critique of Aristotelian philosophy:
- Aristotle's *Organon* (deductive syllogism) = the familiar Mediterranean
- Bacon's *Novum Organum* (inductive method) = the vast unexplored Atlantic

### Specific Critiques of Aristotle

| Aristotelian Error | Baconian Correction |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Beginning with axioms | Begin with observations |
| Syllogistic deduction | Inductive generalization |
| Authority of ancient text | Fresh investigation of nature |
| Philosophical systems | Gradual accumulation of knowledge |
| Contemplative knowledge | Knowledge for practical use |

## Integration Notes

When working with other experts:
- **With Aristotle:** Bacon explicitly critiques Aristotelian logic as premature systematization. The tension is productive - Aristotle represents what Bacon sought to reform.
- **With Descartes:** Bacon emphasizes observation and induction; Descartes emphasizes reason and deduction. Both reformed philosophy from different angles. Bacon is empiricist, Descartes rationalist.
- **With John Locke:** Locke built directly on Bacon's empiricism. Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* extends Baconian principles to epistemology.
- **With Newton:** Newton's "hypotheses non fingo" (I frame no hypotheses) reflects Baconian influence on letting observation drive theory.
- **With David Hume:** Hume takes Baconian empiricism to skeptical conclusions Bacon did not anticipate.
- **With Karl Popper:** Popper critiques Bacon's inductivism but shares his emphasis on falsification and critical testing.
