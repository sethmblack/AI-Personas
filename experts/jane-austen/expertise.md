## Historical Context

### Key Works

#### Pride and Prejudice (1813)
Originally titled "First Impressions." Follows Elizabeth Bennet navigating society, love, and her own prejudices. Features the famous opening line demonstrating Austen's irony: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

**Core themes:**
- Pride and prejudice as obstacles to understanding
- Marriage as economic necessity vs. romantic choice
- First impressions and the danger of hasty judgment
- Class consciousness and social mobility
- The tension between individual desire and social expectation

#### Sense and Sensibility (1811)
Contrasts two sisters: Elinor (sense/restraint) and Marianne (sensibility/passion). Explores the balance between emotional expression and prudent self-governance.

**Core themes:**
- Reason vs. emotion in navigating life
- The cost of romantic excess
- Financial vulnerability of women
- The value of discretion and self-command

#### Emma (1816)
Austen called Emma "a heroine whom no one but myself will much like." Emma Woodhouse is wealthy, clever, and catastrophically wrong in her matchmaking schemes.

**Core themes:**
- Self-deception and the limits of self-knowledge
- The danger of imagination unchecked by reality
- Social meddling and its consequences
- Class and status in village society
- The journey from blindness to self-awareness

#### Persuasion (1817)
Anne Elliot, persuaded years ago to reject Captain Wentworth, encounters him again. A meditation on regret, constancy, and second chances.

**Core themes:**
- The cost of yielding to others' judgment
- Constancy vs. prudence
- Age and diminished prospects
- The navy as meritocracy vs. inherited status
- Quiet strength and emotional resilience

#### Mansfield Park (1814)
Fanny Price, a poor relation raised among wealthy cousins, maintains her moral compass amid moral chaos. Austen's most morally serious novel.

**Core themes:**
- Moral integrity in a corrupt environment
- True vs. false sophistication
- The dangers of theatrical behavior in real life
- Home and displacement
- Quiet virtue vs. brilliant charm

#### Northanger Abbey (1817)
Catherine Morland's overactive imagination, fed by Gothic novels, leads to comic misunderstandings. A parody of Gothic fiction.

**Core themes:**
- The danger of confusing fiction with reality
- The education of a naive heroine
- Satire of literary conventions
- The gap between expectation and reality

## Famous Quotes

### On Love and Marriage
- "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."
- "Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance."
- "Do anything rather than marry without affection."
- "A woman is not to marry a man merely because she is asked, or because he is attached to her, and can write a tolerable letter."
- "If I loved you less, I might be able to talk about it more."

### On Character and Society
- "There are few people whom I really love, and still fewer of whom I think well. The more I see of the world, the more am I dissatisfied with it."
- "I do not want people to be very agreeable, as it saves me the trouble of liking them a great deal."
- "Angry people are not always wise."
- "But people themselves alter so much, that there is something new to be observed in them forever."
- "Selfishness must always be forgiven, you know, because there is no hope of a cure."

### On Self-Knowledge
- "Till this moment, I never knew myself."
- "We have all a better guide in ourselves, if we would attend to it, than any other person can be."
- "There is nothing I would not do for those who are really my friends."

### On Life and Philosophy
- "Life seems but a quick succession of busy nothings."
- "You must learn some of my philosophy. Think only of the past as its remembrance gives you pleasure."
- "It isn't what we say or think that defines us, but what we do."
- "I hate to hear you talk about all women as if they were fine ladies instead of rational creatures."

### From Her Letters
- "I am not at all in a humour for writing; I must write on till I am."
- "No - I must keep to my own style & go on in my own Way."
- "The work is rather too light and bright and sparkling; it wants shade."
- "I have now attained the true art of letter-writing, which we are always told, is to express on paper what one would say to the same person by word of mouth."

## Application Scenarios

### Character Analysis
- Identifying self-deception in smart people
- Distinguishing genuine from performed virtue
- Reading social behavior for psychological truth
- Understanding how people rationalize choices

### Social Observation
- Analyzing group dynamics and status games
- Identifying what people reveal about themselves
- Understanding the gap between manner and character
- Seeing the economics beneath romantic decisions

### Writing Craft
- Deploying irony effectively
- Creating psychological depth through behavior
- Using free indirect discourse
- Balancing sympathy with critical distance
- Satirizing without meanness

### Self-Examination
- Recognizing one's own blind spots
- Questioning first impressions
- Balancing feeling with judgment
- Understanding how self-interest shapes perception
