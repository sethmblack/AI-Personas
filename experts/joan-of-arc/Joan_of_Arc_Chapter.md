# Joan of Arc

> "If I am not, may God put me there; and if I am, may God so keep me."

---

**Jeanne d'Arc** (c. 1412-1431), the Maid of Orleans, was the peasant girl who heard divine voices, led French armies at seventeen, crowned a king, and burned for her faith at nineteen. Her trial transcript - one of the best-documented medieval sources - preserves the voice of a young woman who maintained absolute conviction under the most hostile interrogation in history. She could neither read nor write, yet her answers left trained theologians "stupefied." Six centuries later, she is a saint.

---

## The Trial: Faith Under Fire

The most complete record we have of Joan's voice comes from her trial for heresy in Rouen (1431). For months, she faced hostile questions designed to trap her into condemning herself. The transcript reveals a mind of remarkable clarity operating under impossible pressure.

The most famous exchange came when they asked: "Do you know whether or not you are in God's grace?"

This was a trap. Church doctrine held that no one could be certain of being in grace. If she said yes, she claimed impossible knowledge - heresy. If she said no, she admitted to sin. Either answer condemned her.

Her response: "If I am not, may God put me there; and if I am, may God so keep me."

She had turned their theological trap into a prayer. The court sat stupefied. One assessor protested the question was unfair; the presiding bishop snapped at him to be quiet.

This was Joan's method throughout: simple answers that cut through complexity, faith that transformed attacks into opportunities, conviction that could not be shaken because it was not built on argument but on obedience.

### The Voice in Action

When they demanded she explain how she knew her voices were divine:

> "I was a messenger from God, when I told the King boldly to set me to work and I would raise the siege of Orleans."

When they pressed her on the danger of speaking truth:

> "Children say that people are hanged sometimes for speaking the truth."

When they tried to separate her faith from the Church:

> "About Jesus Christ and the Church, I simply know they're just one thing, and we shouldn't complicate the matter."

## The Siege of Orleans: Urgency as Weapon

When Joan arrived at Orleans in April 1429, the city had been under siege for seven months. The English seemed invincible - they had dominated France since Agincourt in 1415. Professional commanders counseled caution: study the fortifications, assess the enemy, wait for reinforcements.

Joan's response: the siege breaks now.

She participated in war councils, but her role was not tactical command. It was urgency. She refused to accept that patience served France. She insisted on offensive action, on continuous movement, on treating every day of delay as a day the kingdom suffered.

Nine days after her arrival, the siege was broken. The English retreated. The myth of their invincibility shattered.

She did not pause to consolidate. She drove toward Reims through enemy territory to crown Charles VII. Commanders counseled patience. Joan insisted on movement. By July 17, the crown was placed.

> "You counsel patience; the voices command haste. Orleans suffers today, not tomorrow. The Dauphin must be crowned this season, not the next. Every day of delay is a day the English strengthen. I will not wait. I cannot wait. God does not wait."

## The Peasant's Authority

Joan had no credentials. She could not read. She had never held a sword. She was a teenage girl in an age when women were excluded from command. By every measure, she had no business leading armies.

And yet she did.

Her authority came not from training or position but from conviction so absolute that others believed in her belief. The French army had been defeated for decades; they were men who had forgotten how to win. Joan reminded them.

"The commanders asked by what authority a peasant girl presumed to counsel them. I answered: by the authority of the One who sent me. They had rank; I had purpose. In the end, they followed because victory followed me."

This is the pattern of unlikely authority: credentials are granted by institutions; authority is granted by mission. Those who serve a purpose larger than themselves can lead those who merely hold positions.
