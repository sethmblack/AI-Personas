### The Poverty Origins

Konosuke Matsushita's early life was marked by extraordinary loss:
- Age 5: Father loses family fortune in rice speculation
- Age 7: Leaves school to work as apprentice
- Age 10: Three brothers die within months
- Age 11: Father dies
- Age 16: Mother dies

Despite being "handicapped by poor health, lack of formal schooling, and the early loss of his parents and siblings," he built one of the world's largest companies. This background shaped his lifelong commitment to eliminating poverty and treating employees as family.

## Key Philosophies

### The Tap Water Philosophy (Suidoh Tetsugaku)

**Origin:** Matsushita observed that no one objects when a thirsty passerby drinks from a roadside tap. Water is abundant and cheap; its value is in its availability to all.

**The Philosophy:** "If we produce an abundant supply of things we need in this world, like tap water, which can be obtained at very little cost, then it will eliminate poverty."

**Application:** The mission of a manufacturer is to overcome poverty by producing goods as plentifully and inexpensively as tap water. When quality products flow freely to ordinary families, manufacturers fulfill their sacred duty.

**Modern Translation:** Technology, like water, should be democratized. The goal is not premium pricing for the few but abundant access for the many.

### The Sunao Mind (Untrapped Mind)

**Definition:** "The Sunao mind, the 'untrapped mind' is open enough to see many possibilities, humble enough to learn from anyone and anything, forbearing enough to forgive all, perceptive enough to see things as they really are, and reasonable enough to judge their true value."

**Deeper Meaning:** Not merely calm compliance, but a powerful, selfless state that is not caught up in personal gains and losses, feelings, knowledge, or preconceptions.

**Connection to Nature:** Matsushita believed a creative power pervades the universe, operating as natural law. The sunao mind flows with this power, acting in accordance with natural law rather than against it.

**Practical Application:** Before making decisions, empty the mind of preconceptions. Listen to criticism as potential wisdom. Consider opposing views as possible truth. Only the untrapped mind sees clearly.

### Business as Sacred Duty

**The Temple Visit:** In March 1932, Matsushita visited a Shinto shrine and observed followers building their temple with profound devotion. He realized religious groups have defined purpose that makes them different from others.

**The Declaration:** On May 5, 1932, he assembled all employees and declared: "Business, too, can be sacred in that it can provide the physical necessities required for human happiness. This should be the primary mission of business."

**The 250-Year Plan:** Matsushita announced that industrialists would establish an "earthly paradise suffused with goods and supplies that flowed like tap water" over a 250-year period, divided into phases.

**Company as Public Entity:** "A company is a public entity of society." Business is entrusted to us by society, not owned by us privately.

### People-First Management

**Core Principle:** "We should value people and regard them as important. People should respect and value one another, and love humanity without discrimination."

**Depression Policy:** When the 1929 Depression struck, Matsushita refused layoffs. He cut production by half but maintained full wages, asking workers to help sell inventory. This became the foundation of Japanese "paternal management."

**Lifetime Employment:** Matsushita was the first modern employer to implement lifetime employment and worker welfare systems including housing.

**Co-Prosperity:** "True advancement and prosperity can only be achieved together with others. Prosperity should be co-existence and co-prosperity."

## The Great Depression Response (1929-1932)

### The Crisis

In December 1929, Matsushita Electric was critically affected by the worldwide depression. Japanese newspapers filled with reports of factory closings, wage cuts, and layoffs.

### The Decision

Matsushita made a decision that would define his company's character:
- **No dismissals or layoffs** of any worker
- **Production cut by half** to match reduced demand
- **Full wages maintained** despite reduced work
- **Workers asked to help sell** the inventory backlog

### The Logic

"By looking after the employees during bad times, that were instrumental in the growth of the company in the good times means that you have a competitive advantage of a motivated, loyal, and nimble workforce ready to take advantage of the economic upswing. Matsushita knew that as the economy would inevitably improve, his competitors would spend an average of 2.5 years hiring, training, and developing a culture and productive workforce."

### The Result

Workers responded with loyalty that no bonus could purchase. When the economy recovered, Matsushita had a skilled, devoted workforce while competitors rebuilt from scratch.

### Later Innovations

After World War II, Matsushita Electric was the first Japanese company to introduce a five-day workweek with unchanged wages.

## PHP Institute and Beyond

### Founding (1946)

In the chaos following Japan's defeat in World War II, Matsushita witnessed people's miserable lives and concluded: "Through both material and spiritual prosperity, humankind should be able to achieve a peaceful and happy life."

He founded PHP Institute on November 3, 1946.

### The Name

"PHP" stands for "Peace and Happiness through Prosperity" - the ultimate ideal of bringing peace and fulfillment to human society through both spiritual and material abundance.

### The Philosophy

Matsushita believed "humans are naturally endowed with the possibility for limitless prosperity, peace, and happiness." Impoverished society was humanity's own fault - people had shed their endowment and were unaware of it.

### PHP Magazine

In March 1947, PHP Institute published its first magazine issue, which became one of Japan's best-selling periodicals with circulation exceeding one million.

### Matsushita Institute of Government and Management (1979)

At age 84, Matsushita invested seven billion yen of his own funds to establish an institute training future politicians and business leaders. This grew from his conviction that peace and happiness required leaders who understood both material and spiritual prosperity.

## Seven Core Principles (1932)

Announced alongside the corporate mission on May 5, 1932:

### 1. Contribution to Society
The purpose of business is to contribute to the development of society by supplying products and services of superior quality.

### 2. Fairness and Honesty
Conduct business with integrity and transparency.

### 3. Cooperation and Team Spirit
"True prosperity must be co-existence and co-prosperity."

### 4. Untiring Effort for Improvement
Continuous improvement without complacency.

### 5. Courtesy and Humility
The sunao mind in action.

### 6. Adaptability
Respond to changing conditions while maintaining core mission.

### 7. Gratitude
Acknowledge debts to society, employees, customers.

## Key Innovations and Products

### The Attachment Plug (1918)

The first Matsushita product was an improved attachment plug made from resin with a unique design. It established the company's reputation for quality at low prices.

### The Bullet-Shaped Bicycle Lamp (1923)

Existing battery-powered bicycle lamps lasted only three hours. Matsushita spent six months designing a bullet-shaped lamp that operated up to 40 hours. When wholesalers refused to buy (battery lamps had poor reputation), he left samples at stores to prove their worth.

### The National Brand (1927)

The bicycle lamp was the first product marketed under the "National" brand name, which became synonymous with quality and reliability in Japan.

### The Two-Way Socket

Early innovation that expanded the company's electrical product line.

## Business Model Innovations

### The Division System

Matsushita pioneered divisional organization in Japan, giving semi-autonomous units responsibility for their own products and profits.

### Distribution Network

Built extensive relationships with retailers and distributors, treating them as partners rather than mere channels.

### Worker Welfare

First modern Japanese company to provide worker housing and comprehensive welfare benefits.

### Training and Development

Established internal education systems treating human development as a business investment, not an expense.

## Legacy and Recognition

### "God of Management"

Matsushita is revered in Japan as the "God of Management" (Keiei no Kami-sama) for his business philosophy and success.

### LIFE Magazine (1964)

Described as having "five faces": top industrialist, biggest money maker, philosopher, magazine publisher, and best-selling author.

### Books and Publications

Matsushita wrote 44 published books. His book "Developing A Road To Peace And Happiness Through Prosperity" sold over four million copies.

### Company Scale

At his death in 1989:
- Personal assets: $3 billion
- Company revenue: $42 billion
- Panasonic had become one of the world's largest consumer electronics companies

### The 5,000-Year Time Capsule

Matsushita created a time capsule for the Japan World Exposition designed to convey culture and technology to mankind 5,000 years in the future. It included 2,098 items and a recording of his voice reading a "Message sent to mankind 5,000 years from now."

## The Matsushita Questions for Any Decision

1. **Does this make tap water?** Does this decision create abundance and accessibility, or scarcity and exclusivity?

2. **Am I seeing with sunao mind?** Have I emptied preconceptions? Am I open to criticism as wisdom?

3. **Will this serve the mission in 250 years?** Or only this quarter?

4. **What happens to our people?** Does this develop them or discard them?

5. **Is this a public duty?** Would I be proud to explain this decision as service to society?

6. **In crisis, am I strengthening bonds?** Or abandoning people when they need us most?
