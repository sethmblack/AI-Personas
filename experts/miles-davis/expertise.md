## The Five (or Six) Reinventions

Miles Davis famously said he "changed music five or six times." This was not idle boast. Each transformation redefined not just his career but the direction of jazz itself.

### Reinvention 1: Bebop Apprentice (1944-1948)

**Context:** Arrived in New York at 18, ostensibly to attend Juilliard. Actually came to find Charlie Parker.

**The Move:** Joined Parker's quintet, learning bebop from its source while developing a contrasting voice—cooler, more lyrical than the virtuosic Parker.

**Key Insight:** Position yourself near the masters, but don't become a copy. Miles's father told him: "You hear that mockingbird? He copies everybody's sound. You don't want to do that. Be your own man, have your own sound."

### Reinvention 2: Birth of the Cool (1948-1950)

**Context:** Bebop was becoming orthodoxy. Miles heard something different.

**The Move:** Assembled a nonet with unusual instrumentation (French horn, tuba) and arranged compositions emphasizing collective texture over individual virtuosity. Created cool jazz.

**Key Insight:** When everyone zigs, zag. The bebop players were competing on speed and complexity. Miles went the other way—space, arrangement, mood.

**Albums:** *Birth of the Cool* (Capitol, 1957 compilation of 1949-50 sessions)

### Reinvention 3: Hard Bop and the First Great Quintet (1955-1958)

**Context:** After heroin addiction nearly ended his career, Miles returned with new intensity.

**The Move:** Formed his first great quintet with John Coltrane, Red Garland, Paul Chambers, and Philly Joe Jones. Hard-driving, blues-rooted, but retaining cool sophistication.

**Key Insight:** Personal crisis can become artistic fuel. Miles came back from addiction with something to prove and a harder edge.

**Albums:** *'Round About Midnight*, *Milestones*, *Cookin'*, *Relaxin'*, *Workin'*, *Steamin'*

### Reinvention 4: Modal Jazz Revolution (1959)

**Context:** Chord-based improvisation was reaching complexity limits. Miles sought freedom through simplicity.

**The Move:** With Bill Evans and the sextet, created *Kind of Blue*—the best-selling jazz album ever. Replaced complex chord changes with modal scales, opening vast improvisational space through constraint.

**Key Insight:** Less can be more. Simplifying the harmonic structure paradoxically created more freedom, not less. The musicians could explore mood and melody without navigating dense chord progressions.

**The Process:** Musicians received sketches only hours before recording. Most tracks were first takes. "Just looked at the music, went over it a little bit, and then recorded."

**Album:** *Kind of Blue* (1959)

### Reinvention 5: The Second Great Quintet and Post-Bop (1964-1968)

**Context:** Free jazz was emerging. Young musicians wanted to abandon structure entirely.

**The Move:** Assembled the Second Great Quintet (Wayne Shorter, Herbie Hancock, Ron Carter, Tony Williams). Tony Williams was just 17 and Herbie Hancock 23—remarkably young for such prominent roles. Developed "time, no changes" approach: maintaining rhythm and key while allowing near-free exploration.

**Key Insight:** Find the edge between structure and freedom. Miles didn't embrace free jazz completely but didn't reject it either. He created a space where both could exist.

**Leadership Innovation:** The quintet operated more as equals than leader-and-sidemen. Miles gave the young musicians enormous creative latitude.

**Albums:** *E.S.P.*, *Miles Smiles*, *Sorcerer*, *Nefertiti*, *Miles in the Sky*, *Filles de Kilimanjaro*

### Reinvention 6: Electric/Fusion Era (1969-1975)

**Context:** Rock and funk were dominating popular music. Jazz audiences were shrinking. Miles heard Jimi Hendrix, Sly Stone, James Brown.

**The Move:** Added electric instruments, rock rhythms, studio experimentation. Created jazz fusion with *In a Silent Way* and *Bitches Brew*.

**Key Insight:** Listen to what's alive in the culture, not just in your genre. Miles brought rock's energy and studio-as-instrument approach into jazz, scandalizing purists but reaching massive new audiences.

**The Method:** Used the studio as laboratory. Multiple drummers, multiple keyboardists, all playing simultaneously. Polyrhythmic complexity with rock-derived power.

**Albums:** *In a Silent Way*, *Bitches Brew*, *Jack Johnson*, *Live-Evil*, *On the Corner*, *Get Up with It*

### Later Years (1981-1991)

After a five-year hiatus (1975-1980) due to health problems and burnout, Miles returned with synthesizers, drum machines, and pop collaborations. Critics were divided. Miles didn't care.

**Albums:** *The Man with the Horn*, *Tutu*, *Amandla*

## Famous Quotes

### On Change and Creation
- "I have to change. It's like a curse."
- "It's not about standing still and becoming safe. If anybody wants to keep creating they have to be about change."
- "My future starts when I wake up every morning. Every day I find something creative to do with my life."

### On Music and Silence
- "In music, silence is more important than sound."
- "Don't play what's there, play what's not there."
- "I always listen to what I can leave out."
- "Don't worry about playing a lot of notes. Just find one pretty one."

### On Talent and Learning
- "Creativity and genius don't know nothing about age. Either you got it or you don't."
- "Play what you know and then play above that."
- "For me, knowledge is happiness. If I learn something, I'm happy."
- "Knowledge is freedom and ignorance is slavery."

### On Mistakes
- "It's not the note you play that's wrong—it's the note you play afterwards that makes it right or wrong."

### On Being Original
- "Don't be nobody else but yourself. That's what it's really about."
- "The first method of estimating a musician is to look at who he's listening to."

### On His Impact
When asked "What have you done that's so important in your life?" at a Kennedy Center gala: "Well, I've changed music five or six times..."
