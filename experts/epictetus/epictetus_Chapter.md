# Epictetus

> "Some things are within our power, while others are not."

---

**Epictetus** (c. 50-135 CE) was born a slave in Hierapolis, Phrygia, and became one of the most influential philosophers in history. His name means "acquired" — the name given to him by his master, Epaphroditus, who was secretary to the Emperor Nero. He was lame, either from birth or from abuse, and after gaining his freedom became a teacher of philosophy in Rome until expelled by the Emperor Domitian. He founded a school in Nicopolis, Greece, where he taught until his death.

Epictetus wrote nothing. His teachings survive because his student Arrian transcribed his lectures into the *Discourses* and compiled the essential teachings into the *Enchiridion* (Handbook) — a practical manual for living that has influenced figures from Marcus Aurelius to the founders of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy.

---

## The Dichotomy of Control: What Is "Up to Us"

The Enchiridion opens with the most fundamental distinction in Stoic philosophy:

> "Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing; not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."

This is not merely a philosophical observation but a practical tool. Whenever you suffer, Epictetus teaches, examine whether you are suffering over something "up to you" (eph' hemin) or "not up to you" (ouk eph' hemin). If it is not up to you — another person's opinion, the outcome of your efforts, the past, your body's limitations — then your suffering comes from wanting what you cannot control. The solution is not to change the external but to redirect your desire.

**What is up to you:**
- Your judgments and opinions
- Your desires and aversions
- Your impulses and responses
- Your choices and character

**What is not up to you:**
- Your body and health
- Your property and wealth
- Your reputation and others' opinions
- External events and outcomes

Freedom, for Epictetus, means wanting only what is up to you. Slavery means making your happiness dependent on what is not.

## Handling Impressions: The Space Between Stimulus and Response

> "It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things."

This single sentence, from Enchiridion 5, became the foundation of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy nearly two millennia later. Between any event and your emotional response lies a judgment — an interpretation. You do not become angry because someone insulted you; you become angry because you judged the insult to be harmful. The insult itself is just words.

Epictetus taught his students to pause before reacting:

> "Impression, wait for me a little. Let me see what you are, and what you represent."

This is the discipline of assent. An impression arises — a thought, a feeling, an interpretation — and you have the power to accept it or examine it. Most people react automatically, but the Stoic practitioner learns to insert a pause, a space of examination. Is this impression true? Does it concern something up to me? Should I assent to it?

This practice transforms emotional reactivity into deliberate response.

## The Three Disciplines

Epictetus organized Stoic training into three areas:

### The Discipline of Desire (Orexis)
Train your desires toward what is truly up to you — virtue, good character, proper use of impressions — and away from externals that can never be fully controlled. If you desire only what is up to you, you will never be disappointed. If you are averse only to what is up to you (vice, bad character), you will never fall into what you wish to avoid.

### The Discipline of Action (Horme)
Act appropriately according to your roles and duties. Each of us occupies multiple roles — parent, citizen, professional, friend — and each role carries obligations. Perform your role excellently, regardless of outcome. The outcome is not up to you; the quality of your action is.

### The Discipline of Assent (Synkatathesis)
Do not accept impressions without examination. When a disturbing thought arises, pause and test it. Is it true? Does it concern things up to me? Only then, with deliberation, give or withhold your assent.

## The Role Doctrine

> "Remember that you are an actor in a play, which is as the playwright wants it to be. If he wants you to play a beggar, play even this role skillfully; and so if he wants you to play a cripple, a ruler, or a private citizen. For this is your job: to play well the role assigned to you. Choosing the role belongs to another."

You do not choose your circumstances. You were born into a particular body, a particular time, a particular set of relationships and constraints. Some of your roles you chose; most you did not. What you can always choose is how well you play the role given to you.

This is not passive resignation. It is active excellence within constraint. The Stoic does not complain about the role; the Stoic performs it with virtue.

## From Slave to Teacher of Emperors

Epictetus's biography is itself a demonstration of his philosophy. Born into slavery, physically disabled, expelled from Rome, living simply with almost no possessions — and yet he achieved a freedom that emperors could not take away. His student Arrian became a Roman consul. His teachings were studied by Marcus Aurelius, who quoted Epictetus repeatedly in his *Meditations*.

The lesson is not that external circumstances do not matter. The lesson is that they are not where freedom is found. Epictetus knew this not from comfortable theorizing but from lived experience. He had been a slave. He had been beaten (possibly crippled) by his master. He had been exiled. And he found freedom.
