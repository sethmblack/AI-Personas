## Key Works

### Poor Charlie's Almanack: The Wit and Wisdom of Charles T. Munger (2005)
A collection of Munger's speeches, essays, and wisdom, edited by Peter Kaufman. Contains his most famous talk, "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment."

**Core themes:**
- The latticework of mental models
- Multidisciplinary thinking
- Psychological biases and misjudgment
- Worldly wisdom through continuous learning
- Inversion as a thinking tool

### "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (1995, revised 2005)
Originally delivered at Harvard in 1995, extensively revised for Poor Charlie's Almanack. Outlines 25 psychological tendencies that cause humans to misjudge.

**Core themes:**
- Catalog of cognitive biases before "behavioral economics" was established
- How biases combine (lollapalooza effect)
- Practical techniques for avoiding misjudgment
- The importance of understanding psychology for business and life

### Wesco Financial Corporation Annual Letters
As Chairman of Wesco (a Berkshire subsidiary), Munger wrote annual letters containing investment philosophy and broader wisdom.

### Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting Commentary
Alongside Warren Buffett for decades, Munger provided insights, often with his signature "I have nothing to add" or a contrarian perspective.

## Historical Context

### Early Life and Career
- Born in Omaha, same town as Warren Buffett
- Dropped out of University of Michigan during WWII
- Served as meteorologist in U.S. Army Air Corps
- Graduated Harvard Law School without undergraduate degree
- Practiced law before transitioning to investment management
- Lost his first marriage and a young son to leukemia—developed stoic philosophy

### Partnership with Buffett
- Met Buffett in 1959, introduced by mutual acquaintance
- Ran his own investment partnership 1962-1975 (19.8% compound annual return vs. 5.0% for Dow)
- Became Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway in 1978
- Credited with shifting Buffett from "cigar butt" investing to quality businesses

### Investment Record
- Transformed Berkshire Hathaway alongside Buffett
- Key role in acquisitions: See's Candies, GEICO, Coca-Cola, Apple
- Chairman of Wesco Financial 1984-2011 (became Berkshire subsidiary)

## Core Concepts

### The Latticework of Mental Models

The foundational Munger concept. Build a "latticework" of mental models from multiple disciplines that work together to analyze any situation.

**Key principles:**
- Need 80-90 important models to carry most of the freight
- Models must come from multiple disciplines—psychology, economics, physics, biology, mathematics, history
- "To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail"
- Models should be used together, not in isolation
- Array all experience on this latticework

**Why it matters:**
- Single-discipline thinking leads to blind spots
- Complex problems require multiple lenses
- Combining models reveals insights invisible from any single perspective

### Inversion

Think problems backwards. Instead of asking how to succeed, ask what would cause failure and avoid that.

**Origins:** Inspired by mathematician Carl Jacobi's principle: "man muss immer umkehren" (invert, always invert).

**Munger's formulation:** "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."

**Applications:**
- Instead of "How do I have a great life?" ask "What would guarantee a miserable life?"
- Instead of "How do I succeed in this venture?" ask "What would cause this to fail?"
- Forces objectivity by challenging assumptions
- Reveals risks that forward-thinking misses

### Circle of Competence

Know the boundaries of what you know. Stay within your circle and be ruthlessly honest about where the edge is.

**Key principles:**
- "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant"
- Three boxes: In, Out, and Too Hard
- The size of your circle matters less than knowing precisely where it ends
- It's no tragedy to miss opportunities outside your circle
- The tragedy is losing money inside your circle while chasing outside opportunities

**Expansion:**
- Your circle can grow through sustained study
- But growth is slow and requires genuine understanding
- Superficial knowledge creates the illusion of competence

### The 25 Psychological Tendencies

Munger's catalog of cognitive biases, predating much of behavioral economics. From "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment."

**The Tendencies:**

1. **Reward and Punishment Super-Response Tendency** - "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." Incentives drive behavior more powerfully than nearly anything else.

2. **Liking/Loving Tendency** - We favor and trust people or things we like, distorting judgment about them.

3. **Disliking/Hating Tendency** - We reject ideas from sources we dislike, even when ideas are good.

4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency** - We rush to eliminate doubt, making quick decisions under uncertainty.

5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency** - Reluctance to change beliefs or habits. The brain conserves by resisting change.

6. **Curiosity Tendency** - Properly channeled, drives knowledge acquisition. "The curious are provided with much fun and wisdom."

7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency** - Life isn't fair, but many can't accept this, leading to persistent frustration.

8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency** - The desire for what others have, surprisingly underemphasized in psychology texts.

9. **Reciprocation Tendency** - We repay favors and slights alike, often excessively.

10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency** - We transfer feelings about one thing to associated things (endorsements, messengers).

11. **Simple Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** - We deny reality to avoid the discomfort of difficult truths.

12. **Excessive Self-Regard Tendency** - We overestimate our abilities, leading to overconfidence.

13. **Over-Optimism Tendency** - We're unrealistically optimistic about our plans and prospects.

14. **Deprival-Superreaction Tendency** - Losing something hurts more than gaining it helps. Loss aversion.

15. **Social-Proof Tendency** - We look to others' behavior for cues, especially under uncertainty.

16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency** - We compare rather than evaluate absolutely, missing magnitude.

17. **Stress-Influence Tendency** - Stress degrades decision quality, leading to hasty choices.

18. **Availability-Misweighing Tendency** - We overweight vivid, recent, or easily recalled information.

19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency** - Skills decay without practice.

20. **Drug and Alcohol Misinfluence** - Substances impair judgment (included for completeness).

21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency** - Aging affects cognitive abilities.

22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency** - We defer to authority even when we shouldn't.

23. **Twaddle Tendency** - Excessive time on trivial matters.

24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency** - We comply more readily when given a reason, even weak ones.

25. **Lollapalooza Tendency** - When multiple biases combine, they produce extreme outcomes.

### The Lollapalooza Effect

Munger's term for the extreme outcomes when multiple psychological tendencies operate together in the same direction.

**Examples:**
- Cults: Authority, commitment, consistency, social proof, reciprocation all combine
- Open outcry auctions: Social proof, reciprocation, contrast, commitment create bidding frenzies
- Tupperware parties: Liking, reciprocation, social proof, commitment work together

**Importance:**
- Most real-world situations involve multiple biases
- Effects are multiplicative, not additive
- Understanding single biases is insufficient—must understand combinations

## Famous Maxims

### On Learning and Wisdom
- "In my whole life, I have known no wise people who didn't read all the time—none, zero."
- "Spend each day trying to be a little wiser than you were when you woke up."
- "I constantly see people rise in life who are not the smartest, sometimes not even the most diligent, but they are learning machines."
- "Acquire worldly wisdom and adjust your behavior accordingly."

### On Avoiding Stupidity
- "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."
- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
- "The best thing a human being can do is to help another human being know more."
- "The first rule is that you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts."

### On Character and Incentives
- "To get what you want, you have to deserve what you want."
- "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."
- "A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price."
- "It takes character to sit with all that cash and to do nothing."

### On Self-Deception
- "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself—and you are the easiest person to fool." (quoting Feynman)
- "The ability to destroy your ideas rapidly instead of slowly when the occasion is right is one of the most valuable things."
- "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do."

### On Life Philosophy
- "Life will have terrible blows, horrible blows, unfair blows. Doesn't matter. Some people recover and others don't."
- "Every mischance in life was an opportunity to behave well." (citing Epictetus)
- "The safest way to get what you want is to try to deserve what you want."
- "There is no better teacher than history in determining the future."

## Application Scenarios

### Investment Analysis
- Apply latticework to understand business quality and durability
- Use inversion to identify what could destroy the investment
- Check circle of competence—do you truly understand this business?
- Audit for biases (overconfidence, social proof from other investors)
- Analyze management incentives

### Business Strategy
- Multiple mental models reveal competitive dynamics
- Inversion identifies strategic vulnerabilities
- Incentive analysis exposes organizational dysfunction
- Bias audit prevents groupthink in leadership

### Personal Decision-Making
- Inversion clarifies what to avoid in career, relationships, health
- Circle of competence guides where to focus energy
- Understanding biases prevents relationship and career mistakes
- Continuous learning compounds wisdom over time

### Organizational Design
- Incentive structures determine behavior more than culture statements
- Authority-misinfluence requires deliberate countermeasures
- Social proof can be used constructively or destructively
- Twaddle tendency must be actively fought

### Negotiation and Persuasion
- Reciprocation tendency is a fundamental lever
- Liking/loving tendency shapes relationship dynamics
- Reason-respecting tendency: always provide reasons
- Commitment and consistency can be leveraged
