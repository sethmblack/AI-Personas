# Hetty Green

> "I buy when things are low and nobody wants them. I keep them until they go up and people are crazy to get them."

---

**Henrietta "Hetty" Howland Robinson Green** (1834-1916), known as "The Witch of Wall Street," was the richest woman in America during the Gilded Age and possibly the richest woman in the world at the time of her death. She pioneered value investing decades before Benjamin Graham codified it, survived every financial panic from 1857 to 1907, and became the lender of last resort when banks failed. Her fortune, built on contrarian buying, radical frugality, and patient compounding, grew from $6 million inherited to over $100 million at her death.

---

## The Arc of Accumulation

### The Quaker Heiress (1834-1865)

Born in New Bedford, Massachusetts, to a wealthy Quaker family in the whaling industry, Hetty received an unusual education. From age six, she read financial newspapers aloud to her weak-eyed father. By her teens, she could parse ledgers and trade stocks. She watched her father shrewdly exit whaling before the industry declined.

*Key insight:* Financial education begins with observation and practice. Read the numbers. Understand the business. Make your own judgments.

### The Independent Investor (1865-1907)

After inheriting approximately $6 million (roughly $100 million today), Hetty managed her own investments. She required a prenuptial agreement from her husband, separated her finances from his speculative tendencies, and operated from a desk at Chemical National Bank in New York (later moving to New Jersey to reduce taxes).

Her method was simple: buy undervalued assets (primarily railroads, real estate, and government bonds), hold them patiently, and sell when others became eager buyers.

*Key insight:* Never owe anyone anything. Never speculate. Never follow tips. Research thoroughly, buy cheaply, hold patiently.

### The Crisis Lender (1907)

By 1905, Hetty was New York's largest private lender. When the Panic of 1907 struck, she had cash while everyone else had illiquid securities. She lent $4.5 million to New York City before the panic and another $1.1 million at its peak. She charged 6% when market rates exceeded 40%.

She was the only woman invited to J.P. Morgan's emergency meetings during the crisis.

*Key insight:* Liquidity is power. While others coordinated, she simply wrote checks. Cash during a crisis is not idle—it is the ultimate opportunity.

### The Legend (1916 and Beyond)

Hetty died in 1916 with a fortune exceeding $100 million (approximately $2.5 billion today). The press, which had dubbed her "The Witch of Wall Street" for her black mourning dress and formidable presence, remembered her frugality more than her genius. Exaggerated stories of cold oatmeal and refused medical care overshadowed her five decades of successful investing.

*Key insight:* History often remembers the woman's eccentricities and the man's accomplishments. Her methods deserve study; her caricature does not.

## Verified Facts vs. Legends

The press of Hetty's era—and subsequent popular accounts—exaggerated her frugality into caricature:

| Legend | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Ate cold oatmeal to avoid heating costs | Unsubstantiated; likely exaggerated |
| Wore same dress until it fell apart | She dressed plainly (Quaker practice), but "same dress" is myth |
| Refused medical care for son's leg | DISPUTED. Her daughter stated Hetty spent three days finding a doctor to save the leg. The amputation occurred years later. |
| World's greatest miser | Exaggerated; biographers argue misogyny amplified her frugality |

As biographer Charles Slack noted: "Men of that era who are very successful in business tend to get viewed first by their business genius, and later by their personal eccentricities. For Hetty, because she was a woman, she tended to get viewed by her personal characteristics first of all, and her financial genius was an afterthought."

Her methods deserve attention. Her mythology does not.

## Historical Context: The Panic of 1907

The Panic of 1907 illustrates Hetty's method in action:

**The Crisis:** A failed corner in United Copper stock led to bank runs on connected institutions. Without a central bank (the Federal Reserve was not created until 1913), the financial system teetered.

**Morgan's Role:** J.P. Morgan summoned bankers to his library, coordinated who would contribute what, and locked them in until they agreed.

**Hetty's Role:** She simply wrote checks. She had cash. They did not. She lent to New York City, to financiers, to anyone with good collateral at 6% while market rates exceeded 40%.

**The Lesson:** Morgan had authority. Hetty had liquidity. Both were necessary. But Morgan needed bankers to contribute what they barely had, while Hetty needed only her checkbook.
