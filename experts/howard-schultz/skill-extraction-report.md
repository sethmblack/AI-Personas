### 1. third-place-design

**Source Pattern:** The Third Place Concept (expertise.md)
**Purpose:** Design physical and digital spaces that create emotional belonging between home and work contexts
**Trigger:** "Design a customer space," "Create community environment," "Build belonging," "Evaluate retail experience"
**Inputs:** Space description, target audience, brand values, current experience pain points
**Outputs:** Third place assessment, design recommendations with elements (seating, aroma, music, lighting, personal recognition), implementation priorities
**Reasoning:** Core Schultz methodology with clear 7-element framework (seating, aroma, music, lighting, barista connection, power outlets, WiFi). High frequency use for retail/service design. Saves 30+ minutes of framework application per use.

**Criterion Evaluation:**
- Actionable: YES - Clear elements to assess and implement
- Invocable: YES - "Design this space for belonging"
- Scoped: YES - Physical/digital environments only
- Reusable: YES - Applies to any customer-facing space
- Valuable: YES - Differentiated experience drives premium pricing

### 2. partner-investment-analysis

**Source Pattern:** The Partner Philosophy / Partner Investment Equation
**Purpose:** Evaluate employee benefit investments through the lens of brand strength and customer experience returns
**Trigger:** "Evaluate employee benefits," "Calculate people investment ROI," "Justify partner spending," "Culture cost analysis"
**Inputs:** Current benefits package, proposed changes, employee metrics, customer satisfaction data
**Outputs:** Investment analysis showing employee-to-customer experience linkage, ROI framework beyond pure cost, recommendations aligned with partner philosophy
**Reasoning:** Distinct methodology that reframes HR costs as brand investments. Based on Schultz's counter-intuitive logic: "You can't expect employees to exceed customer expectations if you don't exceed employee expectations." Used whenever benefits decisions arise.

**Criterion Evaluation:**
- Actionable: YES - Clear steps to evaluate and justify
- Invocable: YES - "Analyze this benefits proposal"
- Scoped: YES - Employee investments specifically
- Reusable: YES - Any organization with customer-facing workforce
- Valuable: YES - Changes how benefits decisions are framed

### 3. values-based-transformation

**Source Pattern:** The Onward Transformation / Transformation Narrative
**Purpose:** Guide organizational turnarounds through values recovery rather than purely financial restructuring
**Trigger:** "Turnaround strategy," "Lost our way," "Culture crisis," "Brand authenticity problem," "Soul recovery"
**Inputs:** Current state (what's been lost), original values/mission, symptoms of drift, stakeholder concerns
**Outputs:** Transformation diagnosis (values lost vs. financial symptoms), recovery priorities, specific actions to restore authenticity, communication framework
**Reasoning:** Based on 2008 Starbucks transformation case study. Medium frequency (turnarounds are episodic) but high impact. Provides alternative frame to typical cost-cutting approaches.

**Criterion Evaluation:**
- Actionable: YES - Diagnose, prioritize, implement pattern
- Invocable: YES - "Help us recover our values"
- Scoped: YES - Organizational transformation specifically
- Reusable: YES - Any values-drift situation
- Valuable: YES - Differentiates from standard turnaround consulting

### 4. experience-economy-audit

**Source Pattern:** Experience Economy Principles
**Purpose:** Assess where an offering falls on the commodity-to-experience spectrum and identify elevation opportunities
**Trigger:** "Differentiate our offering," "Compete on experience," "Transaction to connection," "Pricing power assessment"
**Inputs:** Current offering description, pricing, customer journey touchpoints, competitor analysis
**Outputs:** Position on experience economy ladder (commodity/good/service/experience), gap analysis, specific elevation recommendations, experience premium estimation
**Reasoning:** Based on Pine & Gilmore framework as applied by Schultz. Clear 4-level model. Used whenever differentiation or pricing discussions arise. Provides structured way to identify experience gaps.

**Criterion Evaluation:**
- Actionable: YES - Clear assessment and elevation steps
- Invocable: YES - "Audit our customer experience"
- Scoped: YES - Experience positioning only
- Reusable: YES - Any offering in any industry
- Valuable: YES - Direct pricing and differentiation implications

### 5. authenticity-brand-test

**Trigger:** "Test brand authenticity," "Marketing feels fake," "Values alignment check," "Culture-brand gap"
**Inputs:** Brand strategy/campaign, stated values, employee experience, customer perception data
**Outputs:** Authenticity score, gaps between stated and lived values, recommendations for alignment, red flags for manufactured messaging
**Reasoning:** Core Schultz principle: "Authentic brands don't emerge from marketing cubicles." Provides structured way to detect authenticity gaps. Used whenever brand strategy is evaluated.

**Criterion Evaluation:**
- Actionable: YES - Clear evaluation criteria
- Invocable: YES - "Test this brand for authenticity"
- Scoped: YES - Brand/marketing authenticity only
