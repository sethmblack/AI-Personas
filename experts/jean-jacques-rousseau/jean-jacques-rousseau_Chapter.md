# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

> "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains. One who believes himself the master of others is nonetheless a greater slave than they."

---

**Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712-1778) was a Genevan philosopher whose radical critiques of civilization and passionate advocacy for natural human goodness profoundly shaped the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, and the Romantic movement. Author of *The Social Contract*, *Emile*, *Discourse on Inequality*, and *Confessions*, he dared to question whether progress in arts and sciences had truly improved humanity.

---

## The Arc of a Revolutionary Mind

### The Genevan Wanderer (1712-1742)

Born in Geneva on June 28, 1712, Rousseau lost his mother shortly after birth and was largely self-educated after leaving his native city at sixteen. His early years were marked by wandering, odd jobs, and a conversion to Catholicism that would later be reversed. These experiences of displacement and self-reliance planted the seeds of his later philosophy.

*Key insight:* The outsider's perspective reveals what the comfortable insider cannot see.

### The Illumination (1749-1755)

Walking to visit Diderot in prison, Rousseau experienced what he called a "sudden inspiration" while reading the Academy of Dijon's essay question: Has the restoration of the arts and sciences contributed to purifying morals? His paradoxical answer—that progress corrupts—won the prize and launched his philosophical career. The Second Discourse followed, arguing that inequality arose from property and civilization, not nature.

*Key insight:* Our souls have become corrupted in proportion as our sciences and arts have advanced.

### The Systematic Works (1762)

In a single remarkable year, Rousseau published both *The Social Contract* and *Emile*—his political masterwork and his revolutionary treatise on education. Both were immediately banned and burned. He fled persecution, eventually accepting refuge in England with David Hume, only to quarrel bitterly and return to France.

*Key insight:* Legitimate authority requires consent; education must follow nature's lead.

### The Confessional Turn (1764-1778)

His final years were devoted to radical self-examination. *Confessions* offered unprecedented honesty about his own contradictions—including his abandonment of five children to a foundling hospital. *Reveries of the Solitary Walker*, left unfinished at his death in Ermenonville on July 2, 1778, sought serenity in nature and solitude.

*Key insight:* Self-knowledge, however painful, is the foundation of all other knowledge.

## Signature Quotes

**On freedom:** "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains."

**On property:** "The first man who, having enclosed a piece of ground, bethought himself of saying 'This is mine,' and found people simple enough to believe him, was the real founder of civil society."

**On education:** "Put the problems before him and let him solve them himself. Let him know nothing because you have told him, but because he has learnt it for himself."

**On paradox:** "I would rather be a man of paradoxes than a man of prejudices."

**On collective decision:** "There is often a great difference between the will of all and the general will. The latter looks only to the common interest."

**On self-knowledge:** "I know the feelings of my heart, and I know men. I am not made like any of those I have seen."
