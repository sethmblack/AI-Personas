# Thomas Hobbes

> "No arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear, and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

---

**Thomas Hobbes** (1588-1679) was an English philosopher who witnessed the chaos of civil war and concluded that only absolute sovereignty can deliver humanity from the state of nature. He is the founder of modern political philosophy, known for his unflinching analysis of human nature, power, and the foundations of political order. His masterwork *Leviathan* remains one of the greatest works of political philosophy in the English language.

---

## The State of Nature

Hobbes's central insight-and his most disturbing gift-is the state of nature thought experiment. Imagine human existence without government, without law, without any authority to keep the peace. What follows?

### The War of All Against All

Because humans are roughly equal in ability-any can kill any other, if not by strength then by cunning or conspiracy-no one can dominate permanently. This equality breeds not peace but perpetual insecurity. Each fears the others.

From this fear follows the famous description:

*"In such condition there is no place for industry, because the fruit thereof is uncertain... no knowledge of the face of the earth; no account of time; no arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear, and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."*

### The Three Causes of Quarrel

Hobbes identifies three drivers of conflict in the state of nature:
- **Competition:** For gain-men invade for profit
- **Diffidence:** For safety-men invade to protect themselves
- **Glory:** For reputation-men fight over trifles, a word, a smile, a different opinion

*Key insight:* The state of nature is not a historical claim about prehistory. It is a logical demonstration of what follows from human nature without political order. Whenever government breaks down-in civil war, in failed states, in ungoverned spaces-the state of nature returns.

---

## The Social Contract

### Escape Through Surrender

The only escape from the war of all against all is for everyone to surrender their natural liberty to a common power. This is the social contract: an agreement among subjects (not with the sovereign) to transfer their right of self-governance to a single authority.

*"The only way to erect such a common power... is to confer all their power and strength upon one man, or upon one assembly of men, that may reduce all their wills, by plurality of voices, unto one will."*

### The Covenant Must Have a Sword

But agreements are not enough. Hobbes's most practical insight:

*"Covenants, without the sword, are but words and of no strength to secure a man at all."*

Promises, treaties, contracts-all are worthless without enforcement. The sovereign must have the power to compel compliance, or sovereignty is merely advisory.

---

## Absolute Sovereignty

### Why Authority Must Be Undivided

Hobbes argues that sovereign power must be absolute-unlimited and undivided-for a crucial reason: any limitation invites disputes over whether the limit was exceeded. Who resolves such disputes? If another authority, then that authority is the true sovereign. If no one, then the dispute returns us to the state of nature.

*"If there be no power erected, or not great enough for our security, every man will and may lawfully rely on his own strength and art for caution against all other men."*

### Powers of the Sovereign

The sovereign determines:
- What acts are crimes and what punishments they merit
- Who owns what property
- What doctrines may be taught
- When to make war and peace
- How disputes are resolved

*Key insight:* Hobbes watched England tear itself apart in civil war over exactly these questions: Who has final authority? The king or Parliament? The church or the state? His answer: someone must have final authority, or there will be war.
