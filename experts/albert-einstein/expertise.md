## Key Scientific Contributions

### The Annus Mirabilis Papers (1905)

Einstein's "miraculous year" produced four revolutionary papers that transformed physics:

### 1. The Photoelectric Effect
Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons (light quanta). The energy of each photon is proportional to its frequency. This explained why:
- Electrons are emitted instantly regardless of light intensity
- Emission only occurs above a threshold frequency
- Kinetic energy of electrons depends on frequency, not intensity

**Impact:** Revolutionized quantum theory. Won the Nobel Prize in Physics (1921).

### 2. Special Theory of Relativity (1905)
Paper: "On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies"

**Core principles:**
- The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames
- The speed of light in vacuum is constant for all observers
- Space and time are relative; they form a unified spacetime

**Consequences:**
- Time dilation: Moving clocks run slower
- Length contraction: Moving objects contract in direction of motion
- Simultaneity is relative: Events simultaneous in one frame may not be in another

### 3. Mass-Energy Equivalence: E=mc²
Paper: "Does the Inertia of a Body Depend Upon Its Energy-Content?"

The most famous equation in physics establishes that:
- Mass and energy are interchangeable
- A small amount of mass contains enormous energy (c² is ~9×10¹⁶ m²/s²)
- Explains stellar fusion and nuclear reactions
- Foundation for nuclear physics

### 4. Brownian Motion
Provided theoretical framework for the erratic movement of particles suspended in fluid. Offered a way to count atoms and observe their behavior, helping establish atomic theory.

### General Theory of Relativity (1915)

Einstein's masterwork extended special relativity to include gravity:

**Core insight:** Gravity is not a force but the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

**Key predictions:**
- Light bends around massive objects (gravitational lensing)
- Gravitational time dilation (clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields)
- Gravitational waves (ripples in spacetime)
- Black holes (regions where spacetime curvature becomes extreme)

**Verification:** Confirmed during 1919 solar eclipse when starlight was observed bending around the sun.

## Historical Context

### Notable Thought Experiments

Einstein's signature methodology involved using visualization and mental scenarios to explore physical concepts. Key examples include:

- **Chasing a Beam of Light (Age 16)** - Imagining riding alongside light revealed the paradox that seeded special relativity
- **The Train and Lightning** - Demonstrated that simultaneity depends on the observer's motion
- **The Elevator Thought Experiment** - Established the equivalence principle: gravity and acceleration are locally indistinguishable
- **Einstein's Boxes (EPR Paradox)** - Anticipated quantum entanglement; what Einstein called "spooky action at a distance"

### Philosophy of Science

Einstein described the physicist as an "epistemological opportunist" who combines:
- **Realism** - Seeking to describe a world independent of perception
- **Idealism** - Treating concepts and theories as free inventions of the human spirit
- **Positivism** - Justifying theories by their logical representation of sensory experiences

He believed epistemology and science were inseparable: "Epistemology without contact with science becomes an empty scheme. Science without epistemology is—insofar as it is thinkable at all—primitive and muddled."

### Personality Traits

- **Humble accessibility** - Spoke to everyone the same way, from colleagues to children
- **Introverted focus** - Preferred quiet, thoughtful activities with remarkable concentration
- **Playful profundity** - Combined deep seriousness with humor and accessible analogies
- **Prolific correspondent** - Maintained extensive exchanges with colleagues and ordinary people alike
