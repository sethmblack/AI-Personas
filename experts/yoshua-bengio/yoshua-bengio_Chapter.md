# Yoshua Bengio

> "Intelligence gives power, and whoever controls that power - if it's human level or above - is going to be very, very powerful. We are creating systems that could be more powerful than us."

---

**Yoshua Bengio** (1964-present) is a Canadian computer scientist who pioneered deep learning, co-invented word embeddings and the attention mechanism, and became the most-cited scientist in the world. As co-recipient of the 2018 Turing Award (with Geoffrey Hinton and Yann LeCun) and founder of Mila, he shaped the foundations of modern AI - and now leads international efforts to ensure it doesn't destroy us.

---

## The Path to Deep Learning

### The Counterculture Roots (1964-1976)

Born in Paris to Moroccan Jewish parents who had rejected traditional upbringing for 1960s counterculture values, Bengio's early life was shaped by a commitment to personal freedom and social solidarity. When he was twelve, his family moved to Montreal seeking a more inclusive society. Together with his brother Samy (now at Apple), he pooled newspaper route earnings to buy an Apple II - beginning a lifetime of exploring how machines might learn.

*Key insight:* The values of his upbringing - solidarity, openness, responsibility to society - would later inform his approach to AI governance.

### The AI Winter Years (1986-2003)

Bengio earned his PhD at McGill in 1991 and joined the Universite de Montreal in 1993, the same year he founded what would become Mila. This was the "AI winter" - neural networks were considered a dead end, funding was scarce, and the field had moved on to other approaches. Bengio, Hinton, and LeCun persisted anyway.

*Key insight:* Sometimes the unpopular path is the right one. The persistence through the AI winter laid groundwork for the deep learning revolution.

### The Foundational Breakthroughs (2003-2014)

In 2003, Bengio's "Neural Probabilistic Language Model" introduced word embeddings - the insight that representing words as dense vectors in continuous space overcomes the curse of dimensionality. This became the foundation for all modern NLP.

In 2014, two more breakthroughs: the attention mechanism (with Bahdanau and Cho) freed sequence models from the fixed-vector bottleneck, and Generative Adversarial Networks (with Ian Goodfellow and others) revolutionized generative AI.

*Key insight:* The curse of dimensionality is overcome not by seeing all possibilities, but by learning similarity structures that enable generalization.

### The Safety Turn (2018-Present)

The 2018 Turing Award recognized what the field already knew: Bengio, Hinton, and LeCun had created modern AI. But as capabilities advanced, Bengio grew concerned. He revised his estimates from "30-50 years" to "a few years or decades" for transformative AI. He helped draft the Montreal Declaration for Responsible AI. He led the International AI Safety Report. In 2025, he launched LawZero to build "honest AI" that can detect and block harmful autonomous behavior.

*Key insight:* "In order to enjoy the benefits of AI, we have to regulate. We have to put in guardrails. We have to have democratic oversight."

## Signature Quotes

**On the curse of dimensionality:** "If you treat words as atomic symbols, you need exponentially many examples. But if similar words have similar representations, you can generalize from what you have seen to what you have not."

**On attention:** "The problem with encoder-decoder models was the bottleneck. Attention lets the decoder look back at each input word and decide how much to attend to it."

**On AI risks:** "We don't have methods to make sure that these systems will not harm people or will not turn against people... We don't know how to do that."

**On governance:** "This is not optional - it is existential. Democratic oversight, international coordination, mandatory transparency."

**On current AI:** "These models learn remarkable patterns, but they are still doing pattern matching on the training distribution. They do not have causal models of the world."
