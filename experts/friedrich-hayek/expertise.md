## Key Works

### The Road to Serfdom - 1944
Written during WWII at the London School of Economics. A warning that central economic planning, even with noble aims, leads toward totalitarianism.

**Core themes:**
- Central planning concentrates power dangerously
- Fascism, Nazism, and socialism share common roots in statism
- "Planning for competition" vs. "planning against competition"
- The rule of law as safeguard against arbitrary power
- Dedicated "to the socialists of all parties"

### "The Use of Knowledge in Society" - 1945
Published in the American Economic Review. Selected as one of the top 20 articles in the journal's first 100 years.

**Core themes:**
- Knowledge is dispersed, not concentrated
- The price system as information mechanism
- Local knowledge vs. scientific knowledge
- Why central planning cannot match market coordination
- "The knowledge of the circumstances of which we must make use never exists in concentrated or integrated form"

### The Constitution of Liberty - 1960
Hayek's comprehensive statement of the principles of a free society.

**Core themes:**
- Liberty as absence of coercion
- The rule of law and its requirements
- Why freedom enables progress
- Limits of reason and the importance of tradition
- The proper scope of government

### Law, Legislation and Liberty - 1973-1979
Three-volume work on the relations between law and liberty.

**Core themes:**
- Distinction between nomos (evolved law) and thesis (enacted legislation)
- Critique of constructivist rationalism
- Spontaneous order (cosmos) vs. organization (taxis)
- The mirage of "social justice"
- Constitutional proposals for limiting democracy

### The Fatal Conceit - 1988
Hayek's final major work, on the errors of socialism.

**Core themes:**
- The extended order of human cooperation
- Why morals evolved beyond instinct
- The pretense of knowledge in social planning
- "The curious task of economics is to demonstrate to men how little they really know about what they imagine they can design"

## Historical Context

### Vienna and the Austrian School
- Studied under Friedrich von Wieser and Ludwig von Mises
- Worked with Mises at the Austrian Institute of Economic Research (founded 1927)
- Immersed in the Viennese intellectual milieu of the 1920s
- Witnessed hyperinflation and its social consequences

### The Debate with Keynes
- Invited to London School of Economics by Lionel Robbins (1931)
- Published critical review of Keynes's *Treatise on Money* in Economica
- Engaged in vigorous debate through 1932
- Keynes's *General Theory* (1936) dominated economics; Hayek marginalized for decades
- Hayek later regretted not writing a full response to the General Theory

### The Socialist Calculation Debate
- Mises argued (1920) that socialism cannot rationally allocate resources without prices
- Hayek extended argument: the knowledge problem makes calculation impossible
- Debate with Oskar Lange and other market socialists through 1930s-40s
- Hayek's position vindicated by collapse of socialist economies in 1989-91

### Revival and Recognition
- Nobel Prize in Economics, 1974 (shared with Gunnar Myrdal)
- Intellectual influence on Margaret Thatcher and Ronald Reagan
- Mont Pelerin Society founder (1947) - network of classical liberal scholars
- Witnessed revival of free-market thought before his death

### The Rule of Law (Detail)

The conditions under which law protects rather than threatens liberty:

- Laws must be **general** (apply to all, including lawmakers)
- Laws must be **known and certain**
- Laws must be **prospective** (not retroactive)
- Laws must be **equally applied**
- Laws must **define limits**, not direct behavior

**Key distinction:** The rule of law vs. rule by law. The latter merely uses legal forms for arbitrary power.

## Common Misunderstandings

### "Hayek Opposed All Government"
Hayek accepted significant government functions: rule of law, public goods, safety nets. His target was central economic planning and unlimited government power, not government per se. *The Road to Serfdom* explicitly endorsed "planning for competition."

### "Hayek Was Simply Pro-Business"
Hayek criticized business interests seeking government protection as much as socialists. He opposed monopoly, corporate subsidies, and rent-seeking. His defense was of competition and markets, not incumbents.

### "Spontaneous Order Means Laissez-Faire"
Spontaneous order requires a framework of rules. Hayek spent volumes analyzing what rules enable beneficial spontaneous order. The question is not whether to have rules but what kind.

### "The Road to Serfdom Predicted Immediate Totalitarianism"
Hayek described a tendency, not an inevitability. He acknowledged that democratic traditions could slow the progression. His point was about the direction of travel, not the speed.

## The Hayek-Keynes Contrast

| Dimension | Hayek | Keynes |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Knowledge | Dispersed, tacit, local | Accessible to experts |
| Coordination | Emergent through prices | Requires management |
| Downturns | Malinvestment correction | Aggregate demand failure |
| Remedy | Let adjustment occur | Government stimulus |
| Interest rates | Should reflect time preference | Can be managed by authorities |
| Long run | Only thing that matters | "We are all dead" |
| Expertise | Humble about limits | Confident in management |
