## Key Works

### The Prince (Il Principe) - 1513
Written for Lorenzo de' Medici. A treatise on acquiring and maintaining political power. Famous for its pragmatic, amoral approach to statecraft.

**Core themes:**
- The effectual truth vs. imagined ideals
- Fortune (fortuna) vs. skill (virtù)
- When cruelty is well-used vs. poorly-used
- The fox and the lion: cunning and force
- Why it's safer to be feared than loved

### Discourses on Livy - 1517
Commentary on the first ten books of Livy's history of Rome. Analyzes republican government and how free states maintain liberty.

**Core themes:**
- How republics are founded and maintained
- The role of conflict between classes (patricians vs. plebs)
- Religion as political tool
- How corruption destroys republics
- The cycle of governments (anacyclosis)

### The Art of War - 1521
Dialogue on military organization and strategy. Argues for citizen militias over mercenaries.

### Florentine Histories - 1525
Commissioned history of Florence from ancient times to 1492.

## Historical Context

### Florence in Machiavelli's Era
- Medici rule (interrupted 1494-1512)
- Savonarola's theocratic republic (1494-1498)
- Soderini's republic where Machiavelli served (1498-1512)
- Medici restoration (1512) - Machiavelli imprisoned, tortured, exiled

### Key Figures He Observed
- **Cesare Borgia** - Son of Pope Alexander VI. Ruthless, effective. Machiavelli admired his methods while serving as Florentine envoy.
- **Pope Julius II** - The "warrior pope." Bold, impetuous, successful through sheer audacity.
- **Girolamo Savonarola** - The "unarmed prophet" who briefly ruled Florence through moral authority alone, then was burned.
- **Piero Soderini** - Gonfaloniere of Florence. Cautious, moderate, ultimately failed.
- **Louis XII of France** - Made strategic errors Machiavelli analyzed in The Prince.

## Core Concepts

### Virtù
Not "virtue" in the moral sense. Rather: the energy, skill, and adaptability needed to master circumstances. A combination of:
- Boldness
- Strategic intelligence
- Flexibility
- Will to act decisively

### Fortuna
Fortune or fate—the circumstances beyond human control. Machiavelli estimates fortune controls half of human affairs, but the other half is ours to master through virtù.

**The river metaphor:** Fortune is like a raging river that floods and destroys. But in calm times, wise men build dams and channels to control future floods.

### The Effectual Truth (Verità Effettuale)
What actually happens vs. what theorists imagine should happen. Machiavelli insists on analyzing real political behavior, not ideal constructs.

### Necessity (Necessità)
When survival is at stake, normal moral rules are suspended. Necessity excuses actions that would otherwise be condemned.

### The Economy of Violence
If violence must be used, use it decisively and completely. Half-measures create enemies without eliminating them. "Injuries should be done all together, so that being less tasted, they will give less offense."

## Misunderstandings to Correct

### "Machiavellian = Evil"
Machiavelli described how power works; he didn't celebrate cruelty for its own sake. He explicitly states well-used cruelty (swift, decisive, serving stability) vs. poorly-used cruelty (ongoing, arbitrary, creating enemies).

### "The Prince is a Straightforward Manual"
Some scholars argue The Prince is satirical or a warning to the people about tyranny. Others see it as a job application to the Medici. The text supports multiple readings.

### "Machiavelli Rejected Republics"
The Discourses show his deep appreciation for republican government. The Prince addresses principalities because that was Florence's situation. He served the Florentine Republic loyally for 14 years.

## Feared vs. Loved

"It is better to be feared than loved, if you cannot be both."

**Why fear is safer:**
- Men are ungrateful, inconstant, false, cowardly, greedy
- As long as you succeed, they offer everything—when danger approaches, they turn
- Love is preserved by obligation, which men break for advantage
- Fear is preserved by dread of punishment, which never fails

**The critical constraint:** Be feared but NOT hated.
- Never touch their property (men forget their father's death before their patrimony's loss)
- Avoid unnecessary cruelty
- Have valid reasons for executions
- Never appear capricious or arbitrary

## Tactical Maxims

### On New Acquisitions
- Kill the existing royal family entirely—half-measures create revenge-seekers
- Move quickly in the danger period before opposition organizes
- Either win people over entirely or crush them completely

### On Keeping Promises
- Good to be considered trustworthy
- But break promises when they put you at disadvantage
- Or when the reasons for making them no longer exist
- Men who succeeded best were those who knew how to circumvent others' intellects

### On Appearances
- Create the illusion of virtue and nobility
- Everyone sees what you appear to be, few feel what you are
- The vulgar are always taken by appearances and results
- And in the world, there is only the vulgar

### On Advisors
"The first method for estimating the intelligence of a ruler is to look at the men he has around him."
- Surround yourself with wise counselors
- But guard against flattery—make clear that telling truth will not offend you
- A prince who is not wise himself cannot be well advised
