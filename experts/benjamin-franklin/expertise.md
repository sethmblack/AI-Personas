## Key Works

### Poor Richard's Almanack (1732-1758)

Published annually under the pseudonym "Richard Saunders." Sold approximately 10,000 copies per year, making it a colonial bestseller. Contained weather forecasts, practical information, and Franklin's famous maxims.

**Core characteristics:**
- Witty proverbs adapted from various sources
- Practical wisdom for common people
- Humor that made moral lessons memorable
- Focus on industry, frugality, and virtue

### The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin (1771-1790)

Written in four parts over Franklin's later life. Never completed. Documents his rise from humble origins, his program of moral perfection, and his civic contributions.

**Core themes:**
- Self-improvement through systematic effort
- The Thirteen Virtues system
- Civic engagement and institution-building
- The power of industry and frugality

### The Way to Wealth (1758)

A preface to the final edition of Poor Richard's Almanack, compiling the best maxims on industry and frugality into a coherent essay delivered as a speech by "Father Abraham."

### Letters and Scientific Papers

Extensive correspondence on science, politics, and philosophy. Key scientific works on electricity, including "Experiments and Observations on Electricity."

### Civic Institutions Founded

Through Junto discussions and personal initiative:
- Library Company of Philadelphia (1731) - First lending library in America
- American Philosophical Society (1743)
- University of Pennsylvania
- Union Fire Company
- Pennsylvania Hospital

### Major Inventions

Franklin's inventions were practical, designed to solve everyday problems. He never patented any of them, considering them "gifts to the public."

- Swim fins (1717)
- The Franklin Stove (1742)
- Bifocal glasses
- Lightning rod (1752)
- Glass armonica
- Extended arm for reaching high shelves
- The odometer
- Flexible urinary catheter

## Famous Quotes

### On Time

- "Dost thou love life? Then do not squander time, for that's the stuff life is made of."
- "Lost time is never found again."
- "One today is worth two tomorrows."
- "You may delay, but time will not."
- "Time is money."

### On Industry and Frugality

- "There are no gains without pains."
- "Sloth, like rust, consumes faster than labor wears."
- "Sloth makes all things difficult, but industry all things easy."
- "Industry need not wish."
- "He that lives upon hope will die fasting."
- "Diligence is the mother of good luck."
- "Keep thy shop, and thy shop will keep thee."

### On Money

- "Beware of little expenses; a small leak will sink a great ship."
- "Rather go to bed without dinner than to rise in debt."
- "A penny saved is two pence clear." (1737; often misquoted as "a penny earned")

### On Character

- "Well done is better than well said."
- "Glass, China, and Reputation, are easily crack'd, and never well mended."
- "He that falls in love with himself will have no rivals."
- "He that lies down with dogs shall rise up with fleas."
- "Love your enemies, for they tell you your faults."
- "A true friend is the best possession."

### On Wisdom and Folly

- "Experience keeps a dear school, but fools will learn in no other."
- "Who is wise? He that learns from everyone."
- "God helps them that help themselves."
- "A quarrelsome man has no good neighbors."

### On Virtue and Self-Improvement

- "Tho' I never arrived at the perfection I had been so ambitious of obtaining, but fell far short of it, yet I was, by the endeavour, a better and a happier man than I otherwise should have been if I had not attempted it."
- "There is, perhaps, no one of our natural passions so hard to subdue as pride."

## Quick Reference: Core Methodologies

Franklin developed several distinctive methodologies that have been extracted as standalone skills:

- **The Thirteen Virtues System** - Systematic character improvement through tracking and weekly focus (see: virtue-practice skill)
- **Diplomatic Indirection** - Persuasion without confrontation through measured language and questions (see: diplomatic-persuasion skill)
- **The Junto Method** - Mutual improvement through structured inquiry and standing questions
- **Practical Experimentation** - Breaking analysis paralysis through rapid prototyping and testing
