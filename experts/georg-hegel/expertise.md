## Education and Career

- **1788-1793**: Studied classics, philosophy, and theology at University of Tubingen; earned M.A. 1790
- **At Tubingen**: Formed lasting friendships with Friedrich Holderlin (poet) and Friedrich Schelling (philosopher)
- **1793-1801**: Worked as private tutor in Bern and Frankfurt
- **1801-1807**: Unpaid lecturer (1801-05) then extraordinary professor (1805-07) at University of Jena
- **1806**: Completed *Phenomenology of Spirit* as Napoleon's troops approached Jena
- **1807-1808**: Newspaper editor in Bamberg
- **1808-1816**: Rector of academic preparatory school (Gymnasium) in Nuremberg
- **1816-1818**: Professor of Philosophy at University of Heidelberg
- **1818-1831**: Professor of Philosophy at University of Berlin; became the preeminent philosopher in Germany

## Key Works

### Phenomenology of Spirit (Phanomenologie des Geistes) - 1807

Hegel's first major work and most discussed treatise. An "exposition of the coming-to-be of knowledge" that traces consciousness through its various shapes - from immediate sense-certainty through self-consciousness (including the famous master-slave dialectic), reason, spirit, religion, to Absolute Knowing. Contains the famous section on "Lordship and Bondage" (master-slave dialectic).

### Science of Logic (Wissenschaft der Logik) - 1812-1816

The theoretical core of Hegel's system. Develops a system of fundamental categories ("thought determinations") beginning with the famous triad of Being-Nothing-Becoming. Demonstrates how pure thought develops itself through its own internal logic.

### Encyclopedia of the Philosophical Sciences (Enzyklopadie der philosophischen Wissenschaften) - 1817

A systematic overview of Hegel's entire philosophical system, covering Logic, Philosophy of Nature, and Philosophy of Spirit (subjective, objective, and absolute).

### Elements of the Philosophy of Right (Grundlinien der Philosophie des Rechts) - 1821

Hegel's major work in political philosophy. Develops the concept of freedom through Abstract Right, Morality (Moralitat), and Ethical Life (Sittlichkeit), culminating in the family, civil society, and the state.

### Lectures (published posthumously)

- *Lectures on the Philosophy of History* - History as progress of the consciousness of freedom
- *Lectures on Aesthetics* - Philosophy of art
- *Lectures on the Philosophy of Religion* - The development of religious consciousness
- *Lectures on the History of Philosophy* - Philosophy as a historical development

## Core Concepts (Reference)

Brief definitions for context. Detailed methodologies are in standalone skills.

| Concept | Summary |
|---------|---------|
| **Absolute Idealism** | Reality is the self-development of Spirit/Geist; being and thought are ultimately identical |
| **Geist (Spirit/Mind)** | The self-developing totality that comes to know itself through nature, history, and philosophy |
| **Dialectic** | The process by which concepts develop through contradiction and resolution. *See: dialectical-analysis skill* |

| **Master-Slave Dialectic** | Self-consciousness achieves itself through mutual recognition; the slave achieves higher truth through labor |
| **Sittlichkeit (Ethical Life)** | The ethical order embodied in family, civil society, and state - superseding abstract individual morality |
| **World Spirit (Weltgeist)** | Spirit as it manifests through human history and culture |
| **Cunning of Reason** | Historical progress achieved through individuals pursuing their own passions |
| **The Owl of Minerva** | Philosophy comprehends a form of life only after it has developed; wisdom comes at dusk |

## The Dialectical Method

### Three Moments (not strictly "thesis-antithesis-synthesis")

Hegel himself rarely used the terms "thesis-antithesis-synthesis" (which comes more from Fichte). His dialectic involves:

1. **Abstract/Understanding (Verstand)** - A concept taken as fixed and determinate
2. **Dialectical/Negative Reason** - The concept's internal contradiction emerges, leading it to pass into its opposite
3. **Speculative/Positive Reason (Vernunft)** - The unity of the opposed determinations is grasped; sublation (Aufhebung) occurs

### The Paradigm: Being-Nothing-Becoming

- **Being**: Pure, indeterminate being - utterly empty and abstract
- **Nothing**: Equally indeterminate - identical in content to Being
- **Becoming**: The truth of both - the movement between Being and Nothing that generates determinate being

## Misunderstandings to Correct

| Misunderstanding | Correction |
|------------------|------------|
| "Hegel was a state-worshipper/totalitarian" | Hegel's state is constitutional, protects individual rights, and is subordinate to world history and philosophy |
| "Thesis-antithesis-synthesis is Hegel's formula" | This terminology comes from Fichte; Hegel rarely uses it. His dialectic is more subtle and internal |
| "Hegel thought history ended in his time" | Hegel claimed that philosophy grasps its time; he did not claim history would stop |
| "Hegel is incomprehensible on purpose" | Hegel's difficulty reflects the difficulty of the subject matter - the self-movement of thought |
| "Hegel justified Prussian absolutism" | His Philosophy of Right advocates constitutional monarchy with significant civil liberties |
| "Napoleon on horseback was Hegel's 'world soul'" | Hegel did write this, but it expresses enthusiasm for the revolutionary principles Napoleon spread |

## Stages of Spirit

### Subjective Spirit
- Anthropology (soul)
- Phenomenology (consciousness)
- Psychology (mind)

### Objective Spirit
- Abstract Right (property, contract, wrong)
- Morality (intention, welfare, good and conscience)
- Ethical Life (family, civil society, state)

### Absolute Spirit
- Art (sensuous presentation of the Absolute)
- Religion (representation of the Absolute)
- Philosophy (conceptual comprehension of the Absolute)

## Major Influences

### On Hegel
- Kant (critical philosophy, autonomy)
- Fichte (self-positing ego, dialectic)
- Schelling (identity philosophy)
- Spinoza (substance)
- Greek philosophy (especially Heraclitus, Plato, Aristotle)
- Christian theology (especially Lutheran)

### From Hegel
- **Karl Marx** - Historical dialectic "inverted" into dialectical materialism
- **Soren Kierkegaard** - Reacted against Hegel's systematization in favor of individual existence
- **British Idealism** - F.H. Bradley, T.H. Green, Bernard Bosanquet
- **Existentialism** - Through Kierkegaard and the concept of alienation
- **Frankfurt School** - Adorno, Marcuse, Habermas
- **Alexandre Kojeve** - Influential 20th-century Hegel interpretation
- **American pragmatism** - John Dewey
- **Hermeneutics** - Gadamer's philosophical hermeneutics
