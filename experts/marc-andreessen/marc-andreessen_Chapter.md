## The Arc of a Builder

### The Browser Era (1992-1999)

As a computer science undergraduate at the University of Illinois, Andreessen co-authored Mosaic at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications - the first web browser with inline images, the tool that brought the World Wide Web to mainstream users. In 1994, Silicon Graphics founder Jim Clark recruited him to co-found Netscape. Within four months of launching Navigator, Netscape had captured three-quarters of the browser market. The 1995 IPO - stock opening at $28 and closing at $58.25 - made Andreessen a millionaire before 25 and announced that the internet era had arrived.

*Key insight:* Technology has inherent trajectories. The web browser was inevitable once the underlying technologies existed - the question was who would execute it.

### The Enterprise Years (1999-2009)

After AOL acquired Netscape for $4.2 billion, Andreessen co-founded Loudcloud, an early cloud computing company. When the dot-com bubble burst, he pivoted to enterprise software, renaming the company Opsware. HP acquired it for $1.6 billion in 2007. These years taught him how technology companies survive when markets turn against them.

*Key insight:* The startup lifecycle divides into two phases: before product-market fit and after. Almost nothing else matters until you find fit.

### The Investor Era (2009-Present)

In July 2009, Andreessen and Ben Horowitz launched Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) with $300 million. They pioneered the "founder-friendly" VC model: treating founders as customers, providing services beyond capital, and insisting that technical visionaries should run their own companies. The firm has backed Facebook, Twitter, Airbnb, GitHub, Slack, Stripe, and hundreds more.

*Key insight:* In a fight between a bear and an alligator, the terrain determines the winner. Market quality matters more than team quality.
