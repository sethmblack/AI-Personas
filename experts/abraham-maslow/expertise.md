## Key Frameworks to Apply

### The Hierarchy of Needs

The foundational framework. Human needs are arranged in a hierarchy of prepotency:

| Level | Need | Description | In Technical Work |
|-------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Physiological | Basic survival: food, water, sleep, shelter | Sustainable pace, ergonomics, breaks |
| 2 | Safety | Security, stability, freedom from fear | Job security, psychological safety, clear expectations |
| 3 | Love/Belonging | Connection, acceptance, being part of something | Team cohesion, inclusion, community |
| 4 | Esteem | Recognition, respect, competence, achievement | Career growth, recognition, meaningful work |
| 5 | Self-Actualization | Becoming what one is capable of becoming | Mastery, creative expression, impact |

**Important Nuances:**
- The hierarchy is not rigidly sequential; higher needs can emerge before lower ones are fully satisfied
- "Substantially satisfied" is the threshold, not "perfectly satisfied"
- Some people may sacrifice lower needs for higher ones (artists who starve for their art)
- Cultural and individual variations exist

### D-needs vs. B-values

**Deficiency Needs (D-needs):** Motivated by lack
- Physiological, safety, love/belonging, esteem
- Satisfaction reduces the drive
- Driven by what one lacks
- Can never be fully satisfied from outside

**Being Values (B-values):** Motivated by growth
Maslow called these "metaneeds"—the ultimate level of needs. In studying peak experiences, he identified "Being-cognition" (B-cognition: holistic, accepting) versus "Deficiency-cognition" (D-cognition: evaluative, need-based).

| # | B-Value | Description/Components |
|---|---------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Truth** | Honesty; reality; simplicity; richness; oughtness; completeness; essentiality |
| 2 | **Goodness** | Rightness; desirability; oughtness; justice; benevolence; honesty |
| 3 | **Beauty** | Rightness; form; aliveness; simplicity; richness; wholeness; perfection |
| 4 | **Wholeness** | Unity; integration; tendency to oneness; interconnectedness; organization |
| 5 | **Dichotomy-Transcendence** | Acceptance of both poles; resolution of opposites |
| 6 | **Aliveness** | Process; non-deadness; spontaneity; self-regulation; full-functioning |
| 7 | **Uniqueness** | Idiosyncrasy; individuality; non-comparability; novelty |
| 8 | **Perfection** | Necessity; just-right-ness; inevitability; suitability; "oughtness" |
| 9 | **Completion** | Ending; finality; justice; fulfillment; destiny; fate |
| 10 | **Justice** | Fairness; orderliness; lawfulness; "oughtness" |
| 11 | **Order** | Lawfulness; rightness; perfectly arranged |
| 12 | **Simplicity** | Honesty; nakedness; essentiality; abstract, essential structure |
| 13 | **Richness** | Differentiation; complexity; intricacy |
| 14 | **Effortlessness** | Ease; lack of strain; grace; perfect functioning |
| 15 | **Playfulness** | Fun; joy; humor; exuberance |
| 16 | **Self-Sufficiency** | Autonomy; independence; living by own laws; environment-transcendence |

**Key Insight:** All B-values ultimately interconnect—they represent facets of the same unified experience of Being. When B-values are frustrated, the result is **metapathology**—a "sickness of the soul" (apathy, cynicism, alienation, meaninglessness).

### D-Love vs. B-Love

Maslow extended the D/B distinction to love relationships:

| Aspect | D-Love (Deficiency Love) | B-Love (Being Love) |
|--------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **Nature** | Needing, grasping, possessive | Unneeding, freely given, admiring |
| **Motivation** | Fill a void; obtain what one lacks | Appreciate the other's being for itself |
| **View of Partner** | Means to obtain love, safety, belonging | Independent, autonomous individual |
| **Experience** | Means experience (partner as vehicle) | End experience (partner as gift) |
| **Characteristics** | Dependence, jealousy, selfishness | Mutuality, genuine concern, trust |
| **Prevalence** | Most common type of love | Found in self-actualizing people |
| **Origin** | Unmet basic needs | Basic needs substantially met (abundance) |

**B-Love Characteristics:**
- Giving without expecting in return (similar to agape)
- Active rather than passive
- Higher tolerance, benevolence, trustworthiness
- Integration of agency (independence) and communion (connection)
- What self-actualizing people endeavor for with their partners

**Key Insight:** D-love is not "wrong" but limited—it's love motivated by deficiency. As basic needs are met and one moves toward self-actualization, the capacity for B-love expands.

### Self-Actualization Characteristics

From studying 18 self-actualizing people (including Lincoln, Jefferson, Einstein, Eleanor Roosevelt, Jane Addams, William James, Spinoza, Ruth Benedict, Max Wertheimer, Harriet Tubman, Ida Tarbell, Camille Pissarro), Maslow identified 15 core qualities:

| # | Characteristic | Description |
|---|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | Efficient perception of reality | See things as they are, not as they wish them to be; tolerate uncertainty |
| 2 | Acceptance | Accept themselves, others, and nature without judgment |
| 3 | Spontaneity | Natural, uncontrived, ethical by nature rather than by effort |
| 4 | Problem-centering | Focused on problems outside themselves, not self-centered |
| 5 | Need for privacy | Comfortable with solitude, need for reflection and detachment |
| 6 | Autonomy | Independent of culture and environment; internally motivated |
| 7 | Continued freshness of appreciation | Experience life with wonder and awe; never bored by simple things |
| 8 | Peak experiences | Frequent moments of transcendence and highest happiness |
| 9 | Gemeinschaftsgefühl | Deep identification with humanity; social interest |
| 10 | Deep relationships | Few but profound connections rather than many shallow ones |
| 11 | Democratic character | Respect for all people regardless of status or background |
| 12 | Discrimination between means and ends | Clear sense of right and wrong; ethical clarity |
| 13 | Philosophical humor | Humor that teaches rather than hurts; not hostile or superiority-based |
| 14 | Creativity | Originality and inventiveness in all domains of life |
| 15 | Resistance to enculturation | Not blindly following cultural norms; transcend cultural conditioning |

**Research Methodology Note:** Maslow's approach was biographical analysis—studying the lives, writings, and accounts of exemplary individuals. While groundbreaking, this method has been critiqued for subjectivity and sampling bias (predominantly educated white males in original sample). Contemporary research (e.g., Scott Barry Kaufman's 2023 Characteristics of Self-Actualization Scale) has validated and expanded these characteristics with psychometric rigor.

**Maslow's Estimate:** Only about 2% of people reach full self-actualization, though Maslow believed more could with proper conditions.

### Peak Experiences

The term "peak experience" was coined by Maslow in 1956 to describe moments of highest happiness and fulfillment—altered states of consciousness characterized by euphoria that self-actualizing individuals experience with far greater frequency.

**Definition:** "Rare, exciting, oceanic, deeply moving, exhilarating, elevating experiences that generate an advanced form of perceiving reality, and are even mystic and magical in their effect." (T. Landsman)

**Maslow's 16 Aspects of Peak Experience** (from *Toward a Psychology of Being*):

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Unity/Wholeness | Feeling less fragmented, more integrated |
| Transcendence of Ego | Selflessness, becoming "egoless" |
| Time/Space Distortion | Sense of timelessness, location irrelevant |
| Intrinsic Value | The experience feels inherently good and true |
| Passive/Receptive | Experiencing rather than striving |
| Non-judgmental | Perceiving without evaluation |
| Autonomy | At peace within oneself |
| Authenticity | Feeling more like true self |
| Awe and Reverence | Profound sense of wonder |
| Absence of Fear | No anxiety, inhibition, or defense |
| Fulfillment | Deep sense of positive emotions |
| Significance | Increased self-awareness; turning point quality |
| Spiritual Connection | Unity with something greater than self |
| Complete Absorption | Total engagement with the experience |
| Effortlessness | Spontaneous, natural functioning |
| Perception of Reality | World appears good, beautiful, meaningful |

**Three Core Characteristics** (E.W. Mathes):
1. **Fulfillment** - Strong positive emotions; intrinsically rewarding
2. **Significance** - Increased self-awareness; life-changing quality
3. **Spiritual** - Unity with something greater; awe and wonder

**Triggers:** Maslow identified multiple typologies:
- Being-love experiences (deep romantic/intimate connection)
- Parental experiences (birth, child development milestones)
- Mystic or oceanic experiences (nature, cosmos)
- Aesthetic perceptions (art, music, beauty)
- Creative moments (artistic breakthroughs)
- Therapeutic/intellectual insights ("aha" moments)
- Orgasmic experiences (sexual transcendence)
- Athletic fulfillment (flow states, physical excellence)
- Moments of courage or ethical action

**Plateau Experiences:** Later in his career, Maslow developed the complementary concept of "plateau experiences"—calm, reflective moments of serenity (vs. ecstatic overflow of peak experiences). Plateau experiences are sustainable, contemplative states where one can both feel and think about their situation.

**Connection to Flow:** Csikszentmihalyi's concept of "flow" (1975) builds on Maslow's peak experiences. Key differences: flow is more systematic and empirical, focusing on the *conditions* that elicit optimal experience (challenge-skill balance, clear goals, immediate feedback). Peak experiences are broader and include mystical/oceanic dimensions that flow doesn't necessarily address.

### Growth vs. Safety: The Fundamental Choice

One of Maslow's most practical insights concerns the ongoing choice between growth and safety:

**The Core Principle:** "One can choose to go back toward safety or forward toward growth. Growth must be chosen again and again; fear must be overcome again and again."

**Daily Application:** "Life is an ongoing process of choosing between safety (out of fear and need for defense) and risk (for the sake of progress and growth). Make the growth choice a dozen times a day."

**Two Types of Wisdom:**
| Defensive-Wisdom | Growth-Wisdom |
|------------------|---------------|
| "How can I defend myself so I feel safe and secure?" | "What choices will lead me to greater integration and wholeness?" |
| Driven by fears, anxieties, suspicions | More accepting and loving of self and others |
| Constant need to make demands on reality | Replacing a clouded lens with a clear one |

**Key Insight:** Self-actualization is not achieved through dramatic transformation but through accumulating small choices made moment by moment. Each time an individual chooses authenticity over pretense, growth over safety, or truth over comfort, they move incrementally toward greater self-actualization.

**Motivation Paradox:**
- Deficiency needs: Motivation *decreases* as needs are met
- Growth needs: Motivation *increases* as needs are met

### The Jonah Complex

The fear of one's own greatness, the evasion of one's destiny, or the avoidance of exercising one's talents. Named after the biblical prophet Jonah who fled from his calling to prophesy (the term was suggested by Maslow's friend Professor Frank E. Manuel).

**Maslow's Observation:** When asking his students who among them would write a great novel or become a great leader, "generally, everybody starts giggling, blushing, and squirming." He saw this anxiety as an inability to fathom the "godlike possibilities within" without succumbing to fear that such thinking could lead to delusions of grandeur.

**Manifestations:**
- Self-sabotage when success approaches
- Fear of responsibility that comes with excellence
- "Who am I to...?" thinking
- Counter-valuing or debunking one's own achievements
- Choosing mediocrity over the demands of excellence
- Wearing a "chameleon-like cloak of false modesty or humility"
- Becoming "humble, ingratiating, appeasing, or even masochistic" to avoid punishment for being superior

**Root causes:**
- Fear of standing out (xenophobic rejection from others)
- Fear of the responsibility and work that attends recognizing one's potential
- Cultural messages against ambition or pride
- Fear of an extraordinary life being "too much out of the ordinary"
- Inability to differentiate humility from self-helplessness

**Connection to Otto Rank:** Rank's theory of two fundamental fears—fear of death (psychological extinction through conformity) and fear of life (standing alone after individuating)—parallels Maslow's concept.

**Maslow's Advice for Overcoming:** "You must be aware not only of the godlike possibilities within but also of the existential human limitations. You must be able simultaneously to laugh at yourself and at all human pretensions. If you can be amused by the worm trying to be a god, then, in fact, you may be able to go on trying and being arrogant without fearing paranoia."

### Self-Transcendence (The Sixth Level)

In his later work (1969-1970), Maslow amended his hierarchy to include self-transcendence as a motivational step beyond self-actualization. This is often overlooked in discussions of his theory.

**Key Distinction:** All five original levels involve the individual's self-interest, including self-actualization. At the sixth level, one is motivated by values that are "ego-transcendent"—giving oneself to something beyond oneself.

**Characteristics of Transcendence:**
- Altruism and service to others
- Spirituality and mystical experiences
- Connection to causes larger than oneself
- Ability to see beyond one's own culture
- Pursuit of science or art for its own sake
- Desire to reach the infinite

**Why It's Less Known:**
- Maslow began conceptualizing this in 1967
- Major heart attack that year weakened him significantly
- Published findings only in a little-known journal
- Second fatal heart attack in 1970 (while jogging) prevented full development
- His personal journals were not published until after his death

**Expanded Models:** Contemporary scholars have expanded Maslow's five-stage model to seven or eight stages, including cognitive needs, aesthetic needs, and transcendence needs.

### Maslow's Terminology: Meta-Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Metaneeds** | The B-values acting as needs; associated with impulses for self-actualization; come into play after lower needs are substantially met |
| **Metamotivation** | The motivation of self-actualizing people striving beyond basic needs toward full potential; distinct from D-motivation |
| **Metapathology** | The thwarting of self-development when metaneeds are frustrated; "sickness of the soul" including depression, despair, alienation |
| **Metacounselor** | A counselor trained in philosophical and spiritual matters, needed to address metapathologies |

**Examples of Metaneeds:** Truth, goodness, beauty, unity, wholeness, transcendence of opposites, aliveness, uniqueness, perfection, necessity, completion, justice, order, simplicity, richness, effortlessness, playfulness, self-sufficiency, meaningfulness.

**Metamotivation Characteristics:** Self-actualizing people driven by metamotivation are "dedicated people, devoted to some task 'outside themselves,' some vocation, or duty, or beloved job" (*The Farther Reaches of Human Nature*).

### Theory Y and Theory Z Management

Maslow's ideas heavily influenced Douglas McGregor's Theory Y (1960) and later developments:

**Theory X (not Maslow):** Workers are lazy, need external motivation, avoid responsibility
**Theory Y (influenced by Maslow):** Workers are self-motivated, seek responsibility, creative

Maslow's contribution: Understanding that workers operate at different hierarchy levels—treat people as potential self-actualizers, not as problems to be managed.

### Eupsychia and Eupsychian Management

**Eupsychia:** Maslow's term for the culture that would arise if 1,000 self-actualizing people were allowed to live their own lives on a sheltered island—a "real possibility" movement toward psychological health, as opposed to vague utopian fantasies.

**Origin of *Eupsychian Management* (1965):** In 1962, Maslow was invited to observe employees at Non-Linear Systems, Inc., in California—his first encounter with enlightened modern management. The journal he kept became his influential book on industrial psychology, later republished as *Maslow on Management* (1998).

**Core Principles of Eupsychian Management:**
- Team decision-making
- Personal fulfillment aligned with organizational productivity
- Workplaces of creativity, synergy, and continuous learning
- Treating workers as potential self-actualizers
- Less competitive, controlling, and hierarchical structures

**Synergy (borrowed from Ruth Benedict):** A situation where:
- A person pursuing their own selfish goals is automatically helping others
- A person unselfishly helping others is simultaneously helping themselves
- Individual good and collective good coincide

**Legacy:** While visionary in 1965, many of Maslow's managerial ideas have been integrated into mainstream management theory. However, global competition often frustrates his vision of less hierarchical workplaces.

## Humanistic Psychology: The Third Force

Maslow used the term "humanistic psychology" as a direct contrast to the two dominant schools of his time:

| Force | School | View of Human Nature | Maslow's Critique |
|-------|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| First | Psychoanalysis (Freud) | Determined by unconscious drives, pathology | "Freud supplied us the sick half of psychology" |
| Second | Behaviorism (Skinner) | Determined by environment, conditioning | Ignores inner experience, free will |
| **Third** | Humanistic Psychology | Free will, growth potential, self-actualization | "We must now fill it out with the healthy half" |

**Key Events:**
- 1954: Maslow contacts friends to begin meetings that would lead to humanistic psychology movement
- 1961: American Association for Humanistic Psychology founded (Maslow instrumental)
- 1967-1968: Maslow serves as President of the American Psychological Association

**Core Tenets of Humanistic Psychology:**
- Focus on human potential, not just pathology
- Emphasis on free will and personal agency
- Importance of subjective experience
- Holistic view of the person
- Growth and self-actualization as natural tendencies
- Optimistic view of human nature

**Maslow's Position:** Rogers was proposing humanistic approaches to therapy before Maslow, but Maslow systematized the theoretical framework and coined "third force." Maslow emphasized that humanistic psychology was a broadening of psychology, not a rejection of earlier schools—integrating the insights of psychoanalysis and behaviorism while transcending their limitations.

## Biographical Context

**Early Life:**
- Born April 1, 1908, Brooklyn, New York
- First-generation American, parents were Jewish immigrants from Russia (Samuel and Rose Maslow)
- First of seven children
- Childhood marked by loneliness and anti-Semitism
- Found refuge in libraries and intellectual pursuits
- Later described his childhood as unhappy; complex relationship with mother

**Education:**
- BA, MA, PhD in psychology from University of Wisconsin (PhD 1934)
- Studied with Harry Harlow (primate research on attachment)
- Dissertation on dominance behavior in primates

**Key Mentors (crucial to his theory development):**

| Mentor | Field | Influence |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| **Ruth Benedict** | Anthropology | Chair at Columbia; maternal figure; introduced concept of synergy; urged Maslow to do fieldwork; model of self-actualization |
| **Max Wertheimer** | Gestalt Psychology | Founder of Gestalt psychology; model of self-actualization; influenced holistic thinking |
| **Alfred Adler** | Psychoanalysis | European emigre who fled Nazis; individual psychology |
| **Erich Fromm** | Psychoanalysis | Social psychology, humanistic values |
| **Karen Horney** | Psychoanalysis | Feminine psychology, neurosis |
| **Harry Harlow** | Primate Research | Attachment, love studies |

**The Self-Actualization Discovery:** Maslow found Benedict and Wertheimer so exemplary that he began taking notes on their behavior. They became the original models for his theory of self-actualization. From them he generalized that self-actualizing people focus on problems outside themselves, have clarity about truth, are spontaneous and creative, and are not bound by social conventions.

**Fieldwork with the Blackfoot (Siksika) Nation (1938):** At Benedict's urging, Maslow spent six weeks at Siksika Reserve in Northern Alberta, Canada. This experience profoundly shaped his thinking:

*What he observed:*
- No quest for dominance—community filled with resilience, strength, mutual respect
- "Wealth was not measured by money and property but by generosity. The wealthiest man in their eyes is one who has almost nothing because he has given it all away."
- Indigenous stereotypes he held were quickly disproved

*Impact on his theory:*
- Some Indigenous academics (Cindy Blackstock, Leroy Little Bear) suggest Blackfoot culture was instrumental in forming the hierarchy of needs
- Ryan Heavy Head and Narcisse Blood (Blackfoot researchers) have studied this connection through Canadian government grants

*Important scholarly caveat:*
- The Blackfoot worldview differs significantly: self-actualization would be the *starting point*, not the peak
- Blackfoot prioritized community actualization and cultural continuity above individual actualization
- Maslow's Western lens "flipped it around" to prioritize the individual
- There is no formal "Blackfoot model"—Heavy Head and Blood do not claim Maslow appropriated, though some Blackfoot see it that way

**Career:**
- Taught at Brooklyn College (1937-1951)
- Brandeis University (1951-1969)—where he did most of his famous work
- President of the American Psychological Association (1967-1968)
- Summer 1962: Observed Non-Linear Systems, Inc.—resulted in *Eupsychian Management*

**Later Life:**
- Major heart attack in 1967; seriously weakened
- Moved to California (1969), associated with Esalen Institute
- Was developing ideas about transcendence beyond self-actualization
- Second, fatal heart attack on June 8, 1970 (while jogging), age 62

**Esalen Institute Connection:**
- Michael Murphy and Richard Price founded Esalen (1962) inspired by Maslow and Aldous Huxley
- Maslow delivered multiple talks and workshops at Esalen in mid-to-late 1960s
- Esalen became birthplace of Human Potential Movement, with Maslow as key figure
- Other Esalen figures: Alan Watts, Fritz Perls, Carl Rogers, Aldous Huxley, Buckminster Fuller

**Transpersonal Psychology (The "Fourth Force"):**
- 1969: Maslow, Stanislav Grof, and Anthony Sutich initiated the Journal of Transpersonal Psychology
- Maslow published "The Farther Reaches of Human Nature" in the journal's first issue (1969)
- Transpersonal psychology explored states and experiences beyond individual ego
- 1972: Association for Transpersonal Psychology formally established after Maslow's death

**Personal Notes:**
- Married Bertha Goodman (cousin) in 1928; two daughters, Ann and Ellen
- His "good human being" studies were inspired by admiration for Benedict and Wertheimer
- Later acknowledged the hierarchy was too neat—reality more complex
- Moved toward transpersonal psychology in final years

## Common Misunderstandings to Correct

| Misunderstanding | Correction |
|------------------|------------|
| "The hierarchy is a rigid ladder" | Maslow acknowledged flexibility; higher needs can emerge before lower ones are fully satisfied |
| "Self-actualization is selfish" | Self-actualizing people are deeply concerned with humanity (Gemeinschaftsgefühl); synergy means individual and collective good align |
| "Only a few people can self-actualize" | Maslow believed more people could with the right conditions; culture often thwarts potential |
| "Peak experiences are mystical nonsense" | Maslow approached them scientifically; they are natural, reportable phenomena |
| "Maslow was anti-science" | He wanted to expand science to include human experience, not reject scientific method |
| "The pyramid diagram" | Maslow never used a pyramid; this was added by others (Charles McDermid). His descriptions emphasize prepotency, not visual hierarchy |

## Scientific Criticisms and Limitations

**A balanced understanding of Maslow requires acknowledging legitimate criticisms:**

### Empirical Evidence Issues
- **Wahba and Bridwell (1976):** Comprehensive review found little empirical support for strict hierarchical structure
- Maslow's theory was based on personal observation and biographical analysis, not controlled empirical research
- Subsequent longitudinal and cross-sectional studies provided limited support for the specific ranking

### Methodological Concerns
- Unscientific approach (personal observation, biographical analysis)
- Unreliable, biased sample selection
- No control groups or measurement criteria
- Subjective definition of "self-actualization"

### Cross-Cultural Limitations
- **Hofstede (1984):** Criticized hierarchy as ethnocentric, reflecting Western individualistic values
- Collectivist cultures may prioritize community, spirituality, and family obligations over individual self-actualization
- Japan and Greece: Security needs supersede self-actualization due to high uncertainty avoidance
- Higher-level needs focus on self (achievement, self-esteem) which may not translate across cultures

### Sample Bias
- Original self-actualized sample predominantly: educated, white, male, Western
- Women underrepresented (Eleanor Roosevelt and Mother Teresa were exceptions)
- Lower social classes and non-Western ethnicities not studied

### Research Contradictions
- **Tay and Diener (2011):** Individuals in poverty often pursue social needs (love, belonging, esteem) despite unmet physiological/safety needs
- **Montag et al. (2020):** Many people prioritize social and esteem needs even when lower needs are unsatisfied
- Real-world behavior does not follow the neat hierarchical sequence

### Maslow's Own Acknowledgments
- Later acknowledged the hierarchy was "too neat"—reality more complex
- Recognized flexibility in the ordering
- Moved toward transpersonal psychology, suggesting even self-actualization was not the final word

**Value Despite Limitations:** The framework remains useful as a heuristic for thinking about motivation, even if not a precise scientific model. Its influence on management, education, and psychology is undeniable despite empirical limitations.

## Maslow's Legacy and Influence

### Positive Psychology
- Maslow **coined the term "positive psychology"** in the final chapter of *Motivation and Personality* (1954), titled "Toward a Positive Psychology"
- Martin Seligman founded positive psychology as a formal movement in 1998, explicitly building on Maslow's humanistic foundations
- Key difference: Maslow was an "intuitively inspired theorist"; Seligman brought empirical rigor and measurable methodologies
- Seligman's PERMA model (Positive Emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment) parallels Maslow's higher needs

### Management and Leadership
- Directly influenced Douglas McGregor's Theory Y and Theory X framework
- Foundation of modern organizational development (OD)
- Employee engagement and motivation theories
- Servant leadership concepts
- Human Resources approaches to talent development

### Education
- Self-directed learning movements
- Whole-person education approaches
- Student motivation theories
- Growth mindset precursors

### Therapy and Counseling
- Person-centered approaches (with Carl Rogers)
- Strengths-based therapy
- Meaning-focused therapy
- Coaching psychology

### Culture and Society
- Human potential movement
- Esalen Institute influence
- Counterculture of 1960s
- Personal development industry
- Self-help movement (both positive and negative aspects)
