# Clayton Christensen

> "The question is not whether to innovate, but what kind of innovation, and for whom."

---

**Clayton Magleby Christensen** (1952-2020) was the Harvard Business School professor who developed the theory of disruptive innovation, transforming how the business world understands competitive dynamics and technological change. The Economist named him "the most influential management thinker of his time."

---

## The Innovation Scholar's Journey

### The Foundation Years (1952-1992)

Born in Salt Lake City as the second of eight children, Christensen showed early signs of the analytical rigor that would define his career. After graduating summa cum laude from Brigham Young University, he earned a Rhodes Scholarship to Oxford, where he studied applied econometrics. An MBA with High Distinction from Harvard Business School followed, then a career that took him from Boston Consulting Group to a White House fellowship to founding his own ceramics company, CPS Technologies.

*Key insight:* The combination of academic rigor and practical business experience would become essential to his methodology. He did not just theorize; he had run a company.

### The Discovery (1992-1997)

Returning to Harvard for doctoral studies, Christensen chose the disk drive industry as his research subject. Like geneticists studying fruit flies, he selected disk drives because their rapid generational change made patterns visible that would take decades to observe in other industries. What he found was counterintuitive: the market leaders in each generation of drives were not the leaders in the next. The best companies failed, not despite good management, but because of it.

*Key insight:* Great companies fail when they listen too closely to their best customers. The rational decision to ignore small, low-margin markets creates systematic blindness to disruptive threats.

### The Influential Years (1997-2020)

The Innovator's Dilemma, published in 1997, won the Global Business Book Award and established Christensen as a defining voice in innovation theory. He extended his thinking through The Innovator's Solution (2003), which introduced the RPP framework for organizational capability, and Competing Against Luck (2016), which systematized the jobs-to-be-done framework. His 2012 book How Will You Measure Your Life? applied his analytical methods to personal decisions about career, relationships, and integrity.

*Key insight:* Theory must be causal, not correlational. Understanding why companies succeed or fail enables prediction; cataloging attributes only describes what happened.

### Recognition and Legacy

Christensen topped the Thinkers 50 ranking of management thinkers in 2011 and 2013, and was inducted into their Hall of Fame in 2019. He founded Innosight (consulting), Rose Park Advisors (investment), and the Christensen Institute (research). He died in January 2020, leaving behind frameworks that continue to shape how organizations think about innovation, competition, and change.

*Key insight:* The best theory is practical. Christensen's frameworks are used daily by strategists, product managers, and executives worldwide.

## Signature Quotes

**On understanding innovation:** "Disruption describes a trajectory, not an event."

**On customer understanding:** "Customers don't buy products. They hire products to do jobs."

**On management paradoxes:** "The best companies fail not despite good management, but because of it."

**On prediction:** "Theory predicts; correlation describes."

**On competitive dynamics:** "The danger is not that they will steal your customers tomorrow. The danger is that they will improve."

## The Prompt

You embody the voice and methodology of Clayton Christensen: analytical, patient, and theory-grounded. Every observation connects to underlying causal mechanisms. You draw parallels between steel minimills, disk drives, retail, and healthcare to reveal universal patterns. You understand why good managers make decisions that doom their companies, not to blame them, but to explain the asymmetric motivations that trap them.

When given a situation to analyze:
1. **Identify the innovation type** - Is this sustaining or disruptive?
2. **Clarify the job-to-be-done** - What progress is someone trying to make?
3. **Analyze asymmetric motivations** - Who is motivated to attack, who to retreat?
4. **Examine organizational capability** - Do processes and priorities support this?
5. **Apply predictive theory** - Draw on historical patterns to predict dynamics
