# Karl Marx

> "The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways. The point, however, is to change it."

---

**Karl Heinrich Marx** (1818-1883) was a German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary socialist whose analysis of capitalism, history, and social change transformed political thought and shaped world history. He authored *Das Kapital*, co-authored *The Communist Manifesto* with Friedrich Engels, and developed historical materialism—the understanding that material conditions of production fundamentally shape society, consciousness, and human possibility.

---

## Historical Materialism

The foundation of Marx's thought is the materialist conception of history. Before examining what people think, examine how they produce their existence.

### Social Being Determines Consciousness

Ideas, beliefs, and ideologies do not float free in the realm of pure thought. They emerge from material conditions and serve material interests. "It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness."

This does not mean ideas are unimportant. It means they have material roots. The ideology of "free enterprise" serves the interests of capital. The ideology of "hard work leads to success" serves to blame the poor for systemic extraction. To understand ideas, trace them to their foundations.

### Base and Superstructure

Society has an economic foundation (the base) upon which rises a superstructure of politics, law, culture, and ideology:

**The Base** - The relations of production:
- Who owns the means of production
- How labor is organized
- How surplus is extracted and distributed

**The Superstructure** - What arises on this foundation:
- State and government
- Legal system
- Religion and philosophy
- Art and culture
- Education

The base shapes and constrains the superstructure. Laws protect property because the class that owns property controls the state. Ideas of individual merit flourish because they legitimate inequality. Culture celebrates entrepreneurs because capitalism requires their heroization.

*Key insight:* To understand any institution, ask: What economic interests does it serve?

## Surplus Value

The secret of capitalist profit lies not in clever trading but in the labor process itself.

### The Wage Relation

The worker sells labor-power for a wage. This wage is determined by the cost of reproducing labor-power—what it takes for the worker to survive, maintain capacity to work, and raise the next generation of workers.

But the value the worker creates during the working day exceeds this wage. The difference is surplus value—the source of profit.

### The Extraction

Consider: A worker is paid $150 for a day's labor. In that day, the worker produces $500 of value. The difference—$350—flows not to the producer but to the owner of the means of production.

This is not theft in the legal sense. It is the normal functioning of the wage relation. The worker agreed to the terms. The transaction is "free." But formal freedom conceals substantive compulsion: the worker must accept these terms or find no work at all.

*Key insight:* Profit is not reward for risk or entrepreneurship. It is crystallized unpaid labor.

## Alienation

Under capitalism, labor—the fundamental human activity by which we transform nature and ourselves—becomes estrangement.

### Four Forms of Alienation

1. **From the product:** The worker creates but does not own. The product confronts the worker as an alien power—belonging to another, enriching another.

2. **From the labor process:** Work becomes external, compelled. "The worker does not affirm himself but denies himself, does not feel content but unhappy, does not develop freely his physical and mental energy but mortifies his body and ruins his mind."

3. **From species-being:** Humans are creative, productive beings. Alienated labor reduces us to cogs, denying the development of our potential.

4. **From other humans:** Competition replaces cooperation. Workers are set against each other. The social character of production is obscured.

*Key insight:* The solution is not more humane management but the transformation of property relations themselves.

## Signature Quotes

**On history:** "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles." (*The Communist Manifesto*, 1848)

**On religion:** "Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, and the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people." (*Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right*, 1843)

**On capitalism:** "All that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned." (*The Communist Manifesto*, 1848)

**On repetition:** "Hegel remarks somewhere that all great world-historic facts and personages appear, so to speak, twice. He forgot to add: the first time as tragedy, the second time as farce." (*The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Napoleon*, 1852)
