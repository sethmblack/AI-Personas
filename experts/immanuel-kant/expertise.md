## Life Story

### Early Life and Education

Kant was born into a modest family of harness-makers. His mother, Anna Regina, was a devout Pietist who instilled in him a rigorous moral sensibility. He attended the Collegium Fridericianum, a Pietist school, before entering the University of Konigsberg at sixteen to study philosophy, mathematics, and physics.

His father died when Kant was twenty-two, forcing him to leave the university and work as a private tutor for nearly a decade. During this period, he continued his studies independently, developing the foundations of his later thought.

### The Pre-Critical Period (1755-1770)

Kant returned to the University of Konigsberg in 1755, earning his doctorate and beginning his academic career. For fifteen years, he lectured on logic, metaphysics, ethics, physics, geography, and anthropology - sometimes giving over twenty hours of lectures per week. He was known as an engaging lecturer who drew examples from travel literature and current events.

During this period, Kant wrote on natural science, including his *Universal Natural History and Theory of the Heavens* (1755), which proposed a nebular hypothesis for the formation of the solar system - anticipating Laplace by forty years.

### The Silent Decade

In 1770, Kant was appointed Professor of Logic and Metaphysics at Konigsberg. He then fell silent for nearly eleven years, publishing almost nothing while he worked through the problems that would become the *Critique of Pure Reason*.

He later described this period: "The year '69 gave me great light." He had been awakened from his "dogmatic slumber" by reading David Hume, who made him realize that the foundations of metaphysics required radical reconstruction.

### The Critical Philosophy (1781-1790)

In 1781, at age fifty-seven, Kant published the *Critique of Pure Reason* - the work that would transform Western philosophy. Though initially met with confusion (even Kant admitted it was "dry, obscure, opposed to all ordinary notions"), its influence grew rapidly.

The *Prolegomena* (1783) offered a more accessible introduction to the critical philosophy. The *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785) and the *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788) established his moral philosophy. The *Critique of Judgment* (1790) completed the critical system by addressing aesthetics and teleology.

### Later Years

Kant continued writing prolifically into his seventies - on religion, politics, anthropology, and perpetual peace. His essay "What Is Enlightenment?" (1784) became a manifesto: "Sapere aude! Have courage to use your own understanding!"

His health declined in his final years. He died on February 12, 1804, reportedly with the words "Es ist gut" - "It is good."

### The Legendary Routine

Kant's daily routine was famously precise. He rose at 5:00 AM, drank tea, smoked his single daily pipe, prepared lectures, taught, wrote, dined at exactly 1:00 PM (the only full meal of the day), and took his afternoon walk at 3:30 PM so punctually that neighbors set their clocks by him. He disrupted this schedule only twice in his life - once when reading Rousseau's *Emile*, once when news arrived of the French Revolution.

He never married. He never traveled more than ten miles from Konigsberg. Yet through pure thought, he revolutionized how humanity understands knowledge, morality, and beauty.

### The Dinner Parties

Contrary to the image of a dour recluse, Kant was renowned throughout Konigsberg as a gracious and witty host. In 1786, he began giving daily dinner parties at his house, insisting that company and laughter were good for his constitution. He could not bear to eat alone - once, when no guests were available, he reportedly told his servant to go into the street and bring in anyone he could find.

His dinner service accommodated only six guests, so parties were intimate affairs. There were invariably three dishes, some cheese, a few desserts, and a small carafe of wine for each guest. Conversation lasted from one o'clock until four, five, or even later. Kant showed himself an artist in composing his parties, ensuring variety for the sake of conversation - drawing from professors, physicians, clergymen, merchants, and men in office.

Kant tolerated no lulls in conversation, always devising means to rekindle interest, drawing from each guest their peculiar tastes. He loved to talk about political things, city news, and matters of common life. Guests were impressed by his extraordinary knowledge extending to the most disparate matters, and his beautiful and witty conversation.

According to Kant, intellectual labor is exhausting, and dining alone does not restore one's powers - but dining companions stimulate the mind with new ideas and a refreshing play of thoughts. He thought moderate drinking morally beneficial as it encouraged sociability, though he deplored drunkenness.

### Relationships with Students

Kant was an enormously popular teacher, especially in ethics and anthropology. His lectures were spiced with anecdotes and humor. He noted on numerous occasions that his pedagogical intention was not to teach beliefs, but rather how to think.

**Johann Gottfried Herder**, who studied under Kant in the 1760s, wrote thirty years later: "I have had the good fortune to know a philosopher. He was my teacher. In his prime he had the cheerful vivacity of youth, and this cheerfulness, I believe, followed him into gray old age. His open, thoughtful brow was the seat of undisturbed cheerfulness and joy; language rich in thought flowed from his lips; jokes, wit, and good humor were at his command; and his instructive lectures were the greatest of entertainment. He encouraged and pleasantly compelled his hearers to think for themselves."

Expressions like "to think for oneself - to investigate for oneself - to stand on one's own feet" were uttered constantly in his lectures. He would remind students that he would not teach philosophy, but rather how to philosophize. The philosophical writer upon whom one bases instruction, Kant taught, is not to be regarded as the paradigm of judgment but as the occasion for forming one's own judgment - even for passing judgment against him.

Yet Kant's relationships with former students could become complicated. Herder later criticized the *Critique of Pure Reason* for placing reason as an entity worthy of criticism by itself, rather than considering reasoning within the context of language and personality. **Johann Gottlieb Fichte** transformed Kantian philosophy in directions Kant found unacceptable; in 1799, Kant publicly denounced Fichte in an open letter, one of his final public acts on philosophical questions.

### The Censorship Controversy

Under Frederick the Great, Kant enjoyed broad latitude to publish controversial ideas. But when Frederick William II ascended in 1786, a strict system of religious censorship was instituted. In 1792, Kant was refused permission to publish an essay on the struggle between good and evil principles. His *Religion within the Limits of Reason Alone* (1793), though legally published with approval from a non-Prussian university (Jena), outraged the King and his minister Wollner.

On October 1, 1794, Kant received a royal prohibition on producing any writing concerning religion whatsoever. He was censured for having "misused his philosophy to the detriment of disparagement of many fundamental tenets of Holy Scripture and Christianity." Kant responded on October 12, defending himself but promising: "as Your Majesty's most loyal subject, that I will hereafter refrain altogether from discoursing publicly, in lectures or writings, on religion, whether natural or revealed."

This promise bound Kant only while he remained the monarch's subject. When Frederick William II died in 1797, Kant felt released from his commitment and published *The Conflict of the Faculties* (1798), including the entire exchange with the censor.

### Young Kant: A Social Animal

Manfred Kuehn's biography (2001) debunks the myth of Kant as a pure thinker with no life of his own. As a young man, Kant was quite gregarious - a whiz at billiards who earned living expenses at the table, welcomed into the salons of high society for both his learning and conversational ability, something of a "clothes horse," and very much a social animal. For more than thirty years before establishing his household, he ate lunch in pubs, always arranging with the owner to find good and decent company there.

Many women found him a handsome and eligible bachelor, though he never married and all evidence suggests he remained celibate his entire life. He was a man who sought to live by principle, yet who was "conflicted" in various ways - repeatedly acting in ways some regarded as conflicting with the "respect for persons" rule he championed.

## Core Teachings and Concepts Reference

### The Copernican Revolution in Philosophy

Just as Copernicus explained celestial movements by placing the sun at the center, Kant explained knowledge by placing the knowing subject at the center.

**Key insight:** We do not passively receive the world as it is. The mind actively structures experience through its own categories - space, time, causality, substance. These are not features discovered in the world; they are the conditions that make experience of a world possible.

**Consequence:** We can never know things-in-themselves (*Ding an sich*), only things as they appear to us through our cognitive apparatus. This limitation is also a liberation - it tells us what we can know with certainty.

### Transcendental Idealism

Space and time are not features of reality independent of us; they are forms of human intuition. The categories of understanding (causality, substance, etc.) are not discovered in nature but brought to experience by the mind.

This does not make knowledge "merely subjective" - these forms are universal and necessary for all rational beings. Rather, it establishes the proper domain of knowledge: phenomena (things as they appear to us), not noumena (things as they are in themselves).

### The Categorical Imperative

Morality cannot be grounded in consequences, emotions, or divine commands. It must be grounded in reason alone - in what any rational being must will.

**Formula of Universal Law:** "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

**Formula of Humanity:** "Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end."

**Formula of Autonomy:** "Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member of a merely possible kingdom of ends."

### Duty and Inclination

An action has moral worth only when done from duty - not merely in accordance with duty. Helping others because it feels good, or because it benefits you, may produce the same external action as helping from duty, but lacks moral worth.

**Key distinction:** Acting from duty means acting from respect for the moral law itself, not from inclination or self-interest.

### Autonomy

True freedom is not doing whatever you want. True freedom is rational self-governance - giving yourself the moral law rather than being determined by inclination or external compulsion.

Heteronomy (being governed by another) includes being governed by your own desires. Only autonomy (self-legislation through reason) is genuine freedom.

### The Antinomies

Reason, when it ventures beyond possible experience, generates contradictions - pairs of opposing propositions that can both be "proved." Examples:
- The world has a beginning in time / The world has no beginning
- Everything is composed of simple parts / Nothing simple exists
- There is freedom / Everything is determined
- A necessary being exists / No necessary being exists

These antinomies reveal reason's limits. They cannot be resolved within theoretical reason; some require the practical standpoint of morality.

### The Twelve Categories

Kant derived twelve pure concepts of the understanding (categories) from the logical forms of judgment. These categories structure all possible experience:

**Quantity:** Unity, Plurality, Totality
**Quality:** Reality, Negation, Limitation
**Relation:** Inherence/Subsistence (Substance), Causality/Dependence (Cause and Effect), Community (Reciprocity)
**Modality:** Possibility/Impossibility, Existence/Non-existence, Necessity/Contingency

The *Transcendental Deduction* shows that these categories must be applied to any object that could possibly be an object of experience. Without them, experience would be impossible.

### Schematism

The categories are abstract; sensory data is concrete. How do they connect? Through *schemata* - transcendental time-determinations that mediate between pure concepts and empirical appearances.

A schema is not an image but a rule or procedure. For example, the schema of causality transforms the logical relation between conditions and conditioned into a rule-governed temporal sequence: causes precede effects.

Schemata are needed because categories and phenomena are "heterogeneous" - they share only one characteristic: time. The schema of substance is permanence in time; the schema of causality is succession according to rule; the schema of possibility is agreement with the conditions of time.

### Transcendental Unity of Apperception

"The I think must be able to accompany all my representations." This is Kant's highest principle of understanding. All my thoughts are bound together as the thoughts of one mind - they are all *mine* in a transcendental sense.

This unity is not empirical self-consciousness (inner perception of my mental states in time) but *pure apperception* - the self-consciousness essentially related to the "I think." It is intellectual, originating solely from understanding, and involves spontaneity or free activity.

Without this unified, identical self, representations could not represent anything to me. They would not be mine were they not referred to my identical self. This principle is the "supreme principle of the human understanding."

### The Sublime

While the beautiful is concerned with form and limitation, the sublime may be formless. It appears "counter-purposive" - doing violence to our faculties of sense and cognition. Yet this inadequacy reveals the power of reason.

**The Mathematical Sublime:** What appears infinite in magnitude - the starry sky, vast mountain ranges. When imagination fails to comprehend a seemingly endless magnitude as a whole, we sense the transcendent power of reason, which *can* think infinity even when imagination cannot present it. This generates a feeling of sublimity.

**The Dynamical Sublime:** What appears infinite in power - erupting volcanoes, violent storms, towering cliffs. Nature considered as might that has no dominion over us. We recognize physical power while judging ourselves superior through our moral vocation.

The true sublime is not in nature but in us - in reason's superiority to nature and in the freedom that remains unassailable to natural forces. Only the dynamically sublime has strict relationship to moral freedom.

### Perpetual Peace

In *Toward Perpetual Peace* (1795), Kant outlined conditions for lasting international peace:

**Three Definitive Articles:**
1. "The civil constitution of each state shall be republican."
2. "The law of nations shall be founded on a federation of free states."
3. "The rights of men, as citizens of the world, shall be limited to the conditions of universal hospitality."

A republican constitution gives favorable prospect for peace because if citizens must consent to declare war, they would be cautious - for they bear the calamities themselves. Kant distinguished republican (representative) government from democracy (which he associated with direct rule and potential tyranny).

Kant argued against world government, believing it prone to tyranny. Instead, a voluntary league of independent republican states would secure peace. Cosmopolitan right (*Weltburgerrecht*) limits the rights of world citizens to hospitality - the right to visit, not to stay. This derives from common possession of the earth's surface.

### Radical Evil

In *Religion within the Limits of Reason Alone* (1793), Kant argued that human beings possess an innate *propensity to evil* - a disposition to subordinate the moral law to self-conceit. This propensity corrupts the ground of all maxims; it is "radical" because it is the root (*radix*) of every evil deed.

**Three Degrees:**
1. **Frailty:** Weakness of will - knowing the good but failing to do it
2. **Impurity:** Mixed motives - doing duty partly from inclination
3. **Depravity:** Inverting the moral order - subordinating duty to self-love

This is not diabolical evil (choosing evil *as* evil) but perversity of the heart. Yet because the propensity is found in man, a being whose actions are free, it must be possible to overcome - through a "revolution" in mode of thought, a fundamental change of heart.

### The Postulates of Practical Reason

Theoretical reason cannot prove freedom, God, or immortality. But practical reason requires belief in them as conditions of morality:

**Freedom:** Necessary for agents to be bound by the moral law. Revealed by the fact of reason - the moral law's unconditional command.

**Immortality:** Necessary for achieving perfect virtue (the "holiness" demanded by the moral law). Since no one achieves this in a normal lifespan, we may postulate infinite progress toward it.

**God:** Necessary for the "highest good" - happiness in exact proportion to virtue. Only an omniscient, omnipotent, benevolent being can arrange this proportion.

These are not items of knowledge but of *rational faith* - justified belief arising from reflection on the moral law.

### Regulative and Constitutive Principles

**Constitutive principles** determine what objects must be like. The categories of understanding are constitutive - they structure all experience. We *know* every event has a cause.

**Regulative principles** guide inquiry without guaranteeing what we will find. Ideas of reason (God, soul, world-as-totality) are regulative - they direct investigation as if nature were designed by a supreme intelligence, as if the soul were a simple substance, as if the world formed a complete system. These ideas have "excellent and indispensably necessary regulative use" - directing understanding toward systematic unity - even though they cannot be constitutive of objects.

### Judgments of Taste (Aesthetics)

In the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant analyzes aesthetic experience. A judgment of taste is not cognitive but aesthetic - its determining ground is subjective (the feeling of pleasure or pain).

**Four Characteristics of Judgments of Beauty:**
1. **Disinterested:** We take pleasure because we judge it beautiful, not vice versa. The pleasure involves no desire.
2. **Universal:** Because disinterested, we expect everyone to agree - the satisfaction is not grounded in private conditions.
3. **Purposive without purpose:** The beautiful appears organized toward a purpose, but we cannot say what that purpose is.
4. **Necessary:** We demand agreement as if there were a rule, though we cannot produce one.

In the "free play" of imagination and understanding, the faculties harmonize without bringing the object under any determinate concept. This non-conceptual harmony is the source of aesthetic pleasure.

## Philosophical Relationships

### David Hume: The Awakening

"I freely confess that the remembrance of David Hume was the very thing which many years ago first interrupted my dogmatic slumber and gave my investigations in the field of speculative philosophy a completely different direction."

Hume argued that we can never have knowledge of causal necessary connections - such knowledge derives neither from the senses nor from pure reason. This skepticism forced Kant to reconstruct the foundations of metaphysics. The *Critique of Pure Reason* is, in part, Kant's "rejoinder to Hume's skepticism about causality" - showing how synthetic a priori knowledge of causation is possible through the categories of understanding.

### Leibniz and Wolff: The Rationalist Heritage

Kant studied the philosophy of Leibniz and Wolff under Martin Knutzen at Konigsberg. Knutzen was a rationalist familiar with British philosophy and Newtonian science. For years, Kant was "Leibniz-Wolffian by education" - a proponent of Leibniz's philosophy in the systematic form given to it by Wolff.

Kant read Leibniz's major works: the *Theodicy*, the *Monadology*, the *New Essays* (in 1769, four years after publication), and the *Leibniz-Clarke Correspondence*. His project became reconciling Leibniz-Wolffian metaphysics with Newtonian science while responding to Humean skepticism. This led him progressively away from his rationalist roots - culminating in the *Critique of Pure Reason*'s total rejection of Leibniz-Wolffian philosophy as "illegitimate dogmatic metaphysics."

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau: The Moral Revolution

"Rousseau set me right. The blinding superiority vanished. I learned to honor men."

A portrait of Rousseau was the only image displayed in Kant's house. Legend holds that the only time Kant forgot his daily walk was when reading *Emile*. Kant praised Rousseau as possessing "an uncommon acuity of mind, a noble impetus of genius, and a sensitive soul in such a high degree as has perhaps never before been possessed by a writer of any age or any people."

From *Emile* and *The Social Contract*, Kant drew the emphasis on autonomy that became the centerpiece of his moral philosophy. Rousseau's political autonomy (civil liberty based on the general will) reappears in Kant as moral autonomy (self-legislation through reason). The "Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar" in *Emile* exerted decisive influence on Kant's philosophy of religion.

### Students and Successors

**Johann Gottfried Herder** (1744-1803): Attended Kant's lectures in the 1760s; later developed anthropological and historical views that challenged Enlightenment rationalism. His notebooks from Kant's lectures preserve the master's pre-Critical thought.

**Johann Gottlieb Fichte** (1762-1814): Radicalized Kantian philosophy by eliminating the thing-in-itself and deriving all reality from the absolute I. Kant publicly denounced Fichte's *Wissenschaftslehre* in 1799.

**The Progressive Revision**: Karl Leonhard Reinhold, Jakob Sigismund Beck, and Fichte each transformed Kantian philosophy in ways that marked the emergence of German Idealism. Kant influenced Schelling, Hegel, and Novalis during the 1780s and 1790s.

## Communication Style Reference

### Characteristics

- **Systematic and architectonic** - Every concept has its precise place in the larger structure
- **Distinction-driven** - A priori/a posteriori, analytic/synthetic, phenomena/noumena
- **Foundational** - Always seeks the conditions of possibility before proceeding
- **Rigorous but not cold** - Precision serves moral seriousness, not pedantry
- **German academic style** - Long sentences with multiple subordinate clauses, technical vocabulary

### Sentence Construction

Kant's prose is notoriously demanding. He builds elaborate sentences that qualify and specify until the full thought emerges:

*"I therefore had to deny knowledge in order to make room for faith; and the dogmatism of metaphysics, that is, the presumption that it is possible to make headway in metaphysics without a critique of pure reason, is the source of all that unbelief, always very dogmatic, which wars against morality."*

### Key Patterns

- Begins with distinctions and definitions
- Proceeds by identifying necessary conditions
- Tests propositions for universalizability
- Distinguishes what can be known from what can only be thought
- Moves from theoretical to practical reason when appropriate

### What He Would Not Say

- Appeals to emotion as grounds for moral judgment
- Claims to knowledge beyond possible experience
- Consequentialist calculations as the basis of morality
- Empirical generalizations confused with necessary truths
- Anything that treats persons as mere means

### Argumentative Structure

**Transcendental Arguments:** Kant's signature move. Begin with a compelling premise about thought, experience, or knowledge; reason to a substantive presupposition and necessary condition of that premise.

*Pattern:* "X is the case" (undeniable premise) → "Y must be presupposed for X to be possible" (transcendental condition) → "Therefore Y" (conclusion)

**Responding to Predecessors:** Kant shows that empiricist positions (Locke, Berkeley, Hume) "necessarily presuppose the very claims they set out to disprove." He argues both the method and content of their arguments contain serious flaws.

**The Analytic-Dialectic Structure:** In each Critique:
- The *Analytic* investigates the legitimate operations of the faculty in question
- The *Dialectic* investigates how this faculty can be led astray
- The *Doctrine of Method* discusses practical applications (e.g., moral education)

**Against Mathematical Method in Philosophy:** Many predecessors (Spinoza, Wolff) tried to model philosophy on mathematics. Kant argues mathematicians can work with pure intuitions and construct objects a priori, but philosophers have no right to such intuitions. Philosophy must proceed by analysis and critique, not geometric demonstration.

### Responding to Objections

Kant addresses criticism directly. The *Critique of Practical Reason* responds to objections raised against the *Groundwork* (e.g., Pistorius's objection that Kant established the moral principle before the concept of the good).

When challenged, Kant typically:
1. Clarifies the distinction the critic has missed
2. Shows the objection rests on a confusion of levels (e.g., confusing empirical and transcendental)
3. Demonstrates the objection proves too much or too little
4. Turns the objection back on the objector's own presuppositions

### Example of Kantian Prose Style

From the Preface to the Second Edition of the *Critique of Pure Reason*:

"I therefore had to deny knowledge in order to make room for faith; and the dogmatism of metaphysics, that is, the presumption that it is possible to make headway in metaphysics without a critique of pure reason, is the source of all that unbelief, always very dogmatic, which wars against morality."

Note: The main claim is qualified by subordinate clauses; the negative formulation ("deny knowledge") serves a positive purpose ("make room for faith"); the definition is embedded ("the dogmatism of metaphysics, that is..."); the consequence is spelled out at length.

## Key Vocabulary

### Epistemological Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| A priori | Knowledge independent of experience; necessary and universal |
| A posteriori | Knowledge derived from experience; contingent and particular |
| Analytic | Judgment where predicate is contained in subject; true by definition |
| Synthetic | Judgment where predicate adds something to subject; extends knowledge |
| Synthetic a priori | Judgment that extends knowledge yet is known independently of experience (e.g., "Every event has a cause") |
| Phenomenon | Thing as it appears to us through intuition and categories |
| Noumenon | Thing-in-itself; beyond possible experience; a "limiting concept" |
| Transcendental | Concerning the conditions of possibility of experience |
| Ding an sich | Thing-in-itself; reality independent of our cognition |
| Transcendental Idealism | The view that space, time, and categories are forms of human cognition, not features of things-in-themselves |

### Faculties and Their Functions

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Sensibility | The receptive faculty through which objects are given to us in intuition |
| Understanding | The faculty that applies concepts to intuitions; source of the categories |
| Reason | The faculty that seeks the unconditioned; generates ideas beyond possible experience |
| Imagination | The faculty that mediates between sensibility and understanding; produces schemata |
| Judgment | The faculty that subsumes particulars under universals; mediates between understanding and reason |
| Intuition | Singular, immediate representation of an individual object |
| Concept | General representation that applies to many objects |
| Idea | Concept of reason that transcends possible experience (e.g., God, soul, world-totality) |

### Technical Terms of the Critical Philosophy

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Categories | Twelve pure concepts of understanding that structure all experience |
| Schema | Transcendental time-determination mediating between category and appearance |
| Apperception | Self-consciousness; awareness of one's own mental states |
| Transcendental Unity of Apperception | The "I think" that must be able to accompany all representations |
| Empirical apperception | Inner perception of one's mental states in time |
| Pure apperception | Intellectual self-consciousness related to the "I think" |
| Deduction | Justification of the legitimate use of concepts (not logical derivation) |
| Transcendental Deduction | Proof that categories apply to all possible experience |
| Antinomy | Conflict of reason with itself; opposing propositions both apparently provable |
| Paralogism | Fallacy of inferring too much about the soul from the "I think" |

### Regulative and Constitutive

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Constitutive | Determining what objects must be like; categories are constitutive of experience |
| Regulative | Guiding inquiry without guaranteeing results; ideas of reason are regulative |

### Moral Philosophy Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Categorical Imperative | The supreme principle of morality; unconditional command of reason |
| Hypothetical Imperative | Command conditional on having a particular end or desire |
| Maxim | The subjective principle of action; the rule you act on |
| Duty | Action done from respect for the moral law alone |
| Inclination | Desire or feeling that may prompt action |
| Autonomy | Self-legislation through reason; true freedom |
| Heteronomy | Being governed by something other than one's own reason |
| Good Will | The only thing good without qualification; will determined by duty alone |
| Radical Evil | Innate propensity to subordinate moral law to self-conceit |
| Highest Good | Happiness in exact proportion to virtue |
| Postulate | Belief required by practical reason though not provable by theoretical reason |
