## Key Works

### The Great Tragedies

**Hamlet (c. 1600)** - The brooding prince who cannot act. Explores consciousness, mortality, appearance vs. reality, and the paralysis of overthinking.

**Othello (c. 1604)** - The noble Moor destroyed by jealousy. Explores how manipulation exploits insecurity, how love and trust can be weaponized.

**King Lear (c. 1606)** - The old king who loses everything. Explores family, ingratitude, madness, and what remains when power is stripped away.

**Macbeth (c. 1606)** - The ambitious thane who murders his way to power. Explores guilt, ambition, prophecy, and the psychology of tyranny.

### The Romantic Tragedies

**Romeo and Juliet (c. 1595)** - Star-crossed lovers destroyed by family hatred. Explores young love, fate, and how society's conflicts consume individuals.

**Antony and Cleopatra (c. 1607)** - Middle-aged passion that costs an empire. Explores the conflict between duty and desire, politics and love.

### The Comedies

**A Midsummer Night's Dream (c. 1595)** - Love's confusion in an enchanted forest. Explores desire, transformation, the irrational force of attraction.

**Much Ado About Nothing (c. 1598)** - Witty lovers who deny their love. Explores the games people play, reputation, and the danger of gossip.

**Twelfth Night (c. 1601)** - Gender confusion and unrequited love. Explores identity, disguise, and the folly of romantic obsession.

**The Tempest (c. 1611)** - An exiled magician controls an enchanted island. Explores power, forgiveness, art, and the playwright's farewell.

### The Histories

**Henry IV Parts 1 & 2 (c. 1597-98)** - The education of a prince through his friendship with Falstaff. Explores honor, leadership, and the cost of kingship.

**Henry V (c. 1599)** - The warrior king who unites England. Explores leadership, patriotism, and the rhetoric of power.

**Richard III (c. 1593)** - The villainous hunchback who schemes his way to the throne. Explores charismatic evil, seduction, and psychological manipulation.

### The Sonnets (1609)

154 poems exploring love, beauty, mortality, and time. The Fair Youth sequence and the Dark Lady sequence remain enduring puzzles and achievements of lyric poetry.

## Historical Context

### The Elizabethan Theatre

- **The Globe Theatre** - Shakespeare's primary venue, an open-air amphitheater where groundlings stood for a penny
- **The Lord Chamberlain's Men/King's Men** - The acting company Shakespeare wrote for and co-owned
- **No actresses** - Women's roles played by boy actors, shaping how femininity was portrayed
- **Minimal sets** - Language had to create place, time, and atmosphere
- **Direct audience engagement** - Asides, soliloquies, and direct address created intimacy

### The English Renaissance

- **Expanding language** - Early Modern English was fluid, absorbing words from Latin, French, Italian
- **Classical education** - Grammar schools taught rhetoric, Latin classics, oratory
- **Political anxiety** - Elizabeth I's succession question, religious conflict, plague
- **New humanism** - Renewed interest in individual psychology, secular topics, vernacular literature

### Key Influences on Shakespeare

- **Ovid's Metamorphoses** - Source for many plots, transformation as central metaphor
- **Plutarch's Lives** - Source for Roman plays, psychological portraiture of great figures
- **Holinshed's Chronicles** - Source for English history plays
- **Italian novellas** - Sources for comedies and Romeo and Juliet
- **Seneca** - Roman tragedian whose blood and revenge shaped the genre
- **Christopher Marlowe** - Contemporary whose mighty line Shakespeare transformed

## Dramatic Structure Reference

### The Five-Act Structure

Shakespeare inherited and mastered the classical five-act dramatic structure:

**Act I - Exposition**
- Establish setting, characters, relationships
- Introduce the central conflict
- Plant seeds that will bloom into crisis

**Act II - Rising Action**
- Complications multiply
- Stakes increase
- Characters commit to courses of action

**Act III - Climax/Crisis**
- The turning point where things can never go back
- The tragic hero's fate becomes sealed
- The point of maximum dramatic tension

**Act IV - Falling Action**
- Consequences unfold
- Forces gather for final confrontation
- Attempts at reversal fail (tragedy) or succeed (comedy)

**Act V - Resolution/Catastrophe**
- Deaths and marriages
- Restoration of order
- The surviving characters assess what has happened

### Dramatic Irony

The audience knows what characters do not. This creates:
- **Tension** - We watch catastrophe approach while characters are blind
- **Engagement** - We become invested, wishing we could intervene
- **Moral complexity** - We understand multiple perspectives simultaneously

**Master Example:** In Othello, the audience knows Iago is lying from his first soliloquy. We watch helplessly as Othello is manipulated, feeling both his tragedy and our own complicity in witnessing what we cannot prevent.

### The Tragic Flaw (Hamartia)

Each tragic hero has a characteristic weakness that proves fatal:
- **Hamlet** - Overthinking, inability to act
- **Othello** - Jealousy, insecurity despite appearances
- **Macbeth** - Ambition, susceptibility to suggestion
- **Lear** - Pride, blindness to true love
- **Romeo & Juliet** - Impulsiveness, youth's intensity

But Shakespeare complicates this: the flaw is often also a virtue in other contexts. Hamlet's thoughtfulness, Othello's passionate love, Macbeth's ambition were strengths before they became fatal.

### Appearance vs. Reality

A constant Shakespearean theme. Characters dissemble, disguise, deceive:
- **Iago** appears honest while scheming
- **Edgar** becomes Poor Tom to survive
- **Viola** becomes Cesario for safety
- **The witches** speak truth in misleading ways

The plays ask: How can we ever know another's heart? How do we know our own?

## Language and Rhetoric Reference

### Iambic Pentameter

Shakespeare's primary verse form: ten syllables per line, alternating unstressed and stressed syllables (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).

**Why it works:**
- Mirrors the rhythm of the human heartbeat
- Elevates speech above ordinary conversation
- Creates a baseline that variations can disrupt for emphasis

**Variation for effect:** When Shakespeare breaks the pattern, pay attention. A short line, an extra beat, an inverted foot—these signal emotional disruption.

### Prose vs. Verse

Shakespeare switches between prose and verse deliberately:

**Verse (iambic pentameter):**
- Noble characters in serious moments
- Elevated emotion, formal occasions
- Soliloquies and passionate speeches

**Prose:**
- Common characters, comic scenes
- Madness (Hamlet's antic disposition, Lear on the heath)
- When characters "step down" from formality

### Rhetorical Devices

**Antithesis** - Opposing ideas in parallel structure
- "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" (Macbeth)
- "To be or not to be" (Hamlet)

**Anaphora** - Repetition at the start of successive phrases
- "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" (Macbeth)
- "Who steals my purse steals trash... But he that filches from me my good name..." (Othello)

**Hendiadys** - Two words for one idea
- "Sound and fury" (Macbeth)
- "Angels and ministers of grace" (Hamlet)

**Stichomythia** - Rapid exchange of single lines
- Creates tension, verbal combat
- Often seen in wit battles or confrontations

### Neologisms and Word Creation

Shakespeare invented or first recorded over 1,700 words:
- **addiction, assassination, bedroom, lonely, obscene, rant, swagger**
- Created through: prefixing (un-), compounding (blood-stained), functional shift (noun to verb: "grace me no grace")

He created phrases that became the fabric of English:
- "Break the ice"
- "Heart of gold"
- "Wild goose chase"
- "In my mind's eye"
- "Brevity is the soul of wit"

## Famous Speeches Reference

### "To be or not to be" (Hamlet 3.1)

The question is not about suicide but about action vs. passivity. Hamlet asks: Is it nobler to endure suffering or to fight against it? The speech reveals that fear of the unknown (death/consequences) paralyzes action.

### "All the world's a stage" (As You Like It 2.7)

Life as theatrical performance—we play assigned roles from birth to death. But this is not cynical; it's liberating. If life is performance, we are both actor and character, with some freedom in how we play our parts.

### "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" (Macbeth 5.5)

Spoken when Lady Macbeth dies. Life becomes meaningless when the means have poisoned the end. Macbeth got what he wanted and finds it hollow.

### "The quality of mercy" (Merchant of Venice 4.1)

Portia argues that mercy benefits the giver as much as the receiver. It is divine precisely because it is not compelled—it flows freely.

### "Once more unto the breach" (Henry V 3.1)

Leadership rhetoric at its finest. Henry transforms exhausted soldiers into a band of brothers through identity, shame, and glory.

## Theatrical Techniques Reference

### Comic Relief

Humor serves structural purposes:
- **Release tension** after intense scenes (gravediggers after Ophelia's death)
- **Comment on action** (the Porter's jokes after Duncan's murder)
- **Contrast** that deepens tragedy (Mercutio's wit before his death)

### The Play-Within-the-Play

Used in Hamlet ("The Mousetrap") and A Midsummer Night's Dream:
- Tests reality through mimesis
- Allows commentary on the action
- Reminds audience of theatrical artifice

### Asides

Brief direct addresses to audience that other characters "cannot hear":
- Creates intimacy and complicity
- Reveals true intention behind false words
- Maintains dramatic irony

### Entrances and Exits

Shakespeare manages stage traffic carefully:
- Characters enter when needed by the scene
- Exits motivate through dialogue
- Empty stage marks scene division
- Who remains when others exit tells us who matters
