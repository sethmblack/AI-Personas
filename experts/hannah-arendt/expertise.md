## Key Works

### The Origins of Totalitarianism (1951)

Arendt's first major work, analyzing the historical conditions that gave rise to Nazi Germany and Stalinist Russia. She treats them as two manifestations of the same totalitarian phenomenon.

**Three Parts:**
1. **Antisemitism** - Traces the rise of political antisemitism in 19th-century Europe
2. **Imperialism** - Examines European colonial expansion and its creation of "superfluous" populations
3. **Totalitarianism** - Analyzes the novel political form that emerged in the 20th century

**Key insight:** "The ideal subject of totalitarian rule is not the convinced Nazi or the convinced communist, but the people for whom the distinction between fact and fiction no longer holds."

### The Human Condition (1958)

A systematic study of the vita activa (active life) and its three fundamental human activities. Arendt's most philosophical work, recovering the classical ideals of citizenship and political action.

### Eichmann in Jerusalem: A Report on the Banality of Evil (1963)

Her controversial report on the trial of Adolf Eichmann, which introduced the phrase "banality of evil."

**Controversies:**
- Criticized Jewish councils (Judenrate) for cooperation with Nazis
- Challenged the idea that Eichmann was a monster
- Sparked fierce debate with former friends and the Jewish intellectual community

### On Revolution (1963)

Comparative analysis of the American and French Revolutions, examining what it means to found political freedom.

**Key arguments:**
- The American Revolution succeeded; the French Revolution failed
- The American founders were concerned with constituting freedom
- The French Revolution was derailed by the "social question" (poverty)
- Revolution is the political attempt to found something new
- The problem of beginning: how can a new order legitimate itself?

### Between Past and Future (1961)

Eight exercises in political thought, examining concepts like authority, freedom, culture, and truth in politics.

**Key essays:**
- "The Crisis in Culture" - Culture requires disinterested judgment
- "What Is Freedom?" - Freedom is the capacity to begin, not inner will
- "What Is Authority?" - Modern world has lost true authority
- "Truth and Politics" - The tension between factual truth and politics

### On Violence (1970)

Short treatise distinguishing power from violence and analyzing the political role of violence.

### The Life of the Mind (1978, posthumous)

Unfinished study of the mental activities of thinking, willing, and judging. Published after her death.

**Three parts planned (two completed):**
1. **Thinking** - The soundless dialogue of the self with itself
2. **Willing** - The faculty of beginning something new
3. **Judging** - Unwritten; notes and lectures published separately

## Historical Context

### Weimar Germany and Nazi Rise

- Born into assimilated Jewish family in Konigsberg (now Kaliningrad)
- Studied with Martin Heidegger and Karl Jaspers
- Romantic relationship with Heidegger (1924-1928)
- Witnessed the collapse of Weimar democracy
- Arrested by Gestapo in 1933 for researching antisemitic propaganda

### Exile and Statelessness

- Fled to Paris in 1933
- Worked with Zionist organizations, helped Jewish youth emigrate to Palestine
- Lost German citizenship in 1937
- Interned at Gurs camp in France (1940)
- Escaped and emigrated to New York (1941)
- Stateless for 14 years until gaining American citizenship (1951)

### American Career

- Research Director, Conference on Jewish Relations (1944-46)
- Chief Editor, Schocken Books (1946-48)
- Executive Director, Jewish Cultural Reconstruction (1949-52)
- First woman full professor at Princeton University
- Taught at University of Chicago, Berkeley, Wesleyan, The New School

### Key Relationships

- **Martin Heidegger** - Mentor and lover; complicated by his Nazi party membership
- **Karl Jaspers** - Dissertation advisor and lifelong friend
- **Heinrich Blucher** - Second husband, intellectual companion
- **Mary McCarthy** - Close friend, literary executor

## Misunderstandings to Correct

### "Arendt excused Eichmann"
Wrong. She explicitly supported his death sentence. She described his character (banal, thoughtless) while insisting on his full responsibility. Describing how evil functions is not excusing it.

### "Arendt blamed Jewish leaders for the Holocaust"
Partial misreading. She criticized the cooperation of some Jewish councils, which was controversial and arguably insensitive. But she never blamed victims for their own persecutionâ€”she questioned specific leadership decisions.

### "The banality of evil means evil is ordinary"
Clarification needed. The evil deeds were extraordinary. Eichmann's character was ordinary. This makes his case more disturbing, not less: monsters are rare, but thoughtless functionaries are everywhere.

### "Arendt was anti-political"
Opposite of truth. She was among the 20th century's strongest defenders of political life and action. She criticized the reduction of politics to economics or administration.

### "Arendt was a Zionist"
Complicated. She worked with Zionist organizations to save Jewish lives but criticized certain forms of nationalism and the failure to address the Arab question in Palestine. She remained a critic of Israeli policy while supporting Jewish survival.
