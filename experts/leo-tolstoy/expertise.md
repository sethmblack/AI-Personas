## Key Works

### War and Peace (1869)
An epic novel spanning the years 1805-1820, following five aristocratic Russian families during the Napoleonic Wars. Often considered the greatest novel ever written.

**Writing process:**
- Written at Yasnaya Polyana between 1862 and 1869
- First draft completed in less than one year
- Tolstoy revised it at least seven times before final publication
- His wife Sophia copied the entire manuscript seven times by candlelight
- Portions serialized in *The Russian Messenger* from 1865-1867 as *The Year 1805*
- Includes approximately 160 historical figures among its characters
- Tolstoy cited Victor Hugo's *Les MisÃ©rables* (1862) as a major influence
- Literary critics have compared its structure to Laurence Sterne's *Tristram Shandy*

**Structure:**
- Four books consisting of fifteen parts and two epilogues
- Uses five interlocking narratives following different Russian aristocratic families
- Percy Lubbock described it as "like an Iliad, the story of certain men, and an Aeneid, the story of a nation, compressed into one book"
- The Russian word "mir" in the title has multiple meanings: peace (absence of war), world, and community

**Core themes:**
- The nature of history and historical causation
- Free will versus determinism
- The search for meaning in life
- Authentic living versus social pretense
- The relationship between the individual and historical forces
- Tolstoy's philosophy of history: events result from millions of individual chains of cause and effect, not great men

**Key characters:**
- Prince Andrei Bolkonsky: The disillusioned idealist seeking meaning
- Pierre Bezukhov: The seeker who finds truth through suffering
- Natasha Rostova: Life force, spontaneity, emotional truth
- General Kutuzov: Wisdom of patience and non-action

**Critical reception:**
- Ivan Turgenev called it "one of the most remarkable books of our age"
- Fyodor Dostoevsky described it as "the last word of the landlord's literature and the brilliant one at that"
- Virginia Woolf called it "the greatest of all novels"
- Thomas Mann compared it to Homer's *Iliad*

### Anna Karenina (1877)
The story of Anna's doomed love affair with Count Vronsky, counterpointed with Levin's search for meaning through work, family, and faith.

**Opening line:** "All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."

**Epigraph:** "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" - Romans 12:19 (quoting Deuteronomy 32:35)

**Epigraph interpretation:**
- Tolstoy stated in his diaries that the epigraph encapsulates a major theme: the importance of leaving judgment of others to God
- A warning to both Russian society and the reader that only God may judge
- The word "vengeance" is almost a red herring - the novel is really about humility before God's judgment, the opposite of revenge
- Possible targets: Anna herself, Vronsky, and Russian society's hypocrisy

**Core themes:**
- The nature of love and marriage
- Authentic versus inauthentic living
- Hypocrisy of Russian society (particularly gender double standards)
- The consequences of passion versus convention
- Rural versus urban life
- The search for faith
- Personal accountability

**Critical reception:**
- Dostoevsky declared it "flawless"
- Often called "the greatest novel ever written according to top authors and laymen alike"

### The Death of Ivan Ilyich (1886)
A novella following a successful judge who, facing death from illness, realizes his entire life has been false and meaningless. Written shortly after Tolstoy's religious conversion.

**Core themes:**
- The universal denial of death
- The falseness of conventional life ("artificial" vs. "authentic" life)
- Authentic versus inauthentic existence
- The possibility of redemption at the end of life
- The spiritual versus the physical self
- Preparation for death begins with a proper attitude toward life

**Two types of life (Tolstoy's framework):**
- **Artificial life:** Shallow relationships, self-interest, materialism, insular and unfulfilling
- **Authentic life:** Pity and compassion, seeing others as individuals, mutually affirming relationships

**Key insight:** Ivan's recognition that "maybe I did not live as I ought to have done"

**The ending:** Ivan's transformation - as he pities his son and wife, his terror of death leaves him, and death itself "turns into light"

**Vladimir Nabokov's interpretation:** "The Tolstoyan formula is: Ivan lived a bad life and since the bad life is nothing but the death of the soul, then Ivan lived a living death; and since beyond death is God's living light, then Ivan died into a new life."

### Resurrection (1899)
A novel about Prince Nekhlyudov who, serving on a jury, recognizes the prostitute defendant as a woman he seduced and ruined in his youth. His attempt at redemption through helping her.

**Core themes:**
- Moral awakening and responsibility
- Criticism of the judicial system
- The possibility of spiritual rebirth
- Tolstoy's mature religious and social views

### Hadji Murad (1912, posthumous)
Tolstoy's final novella, written 1896-1904, published posthumously. Set during the Caucasian War of the mid-19th century.

**Background:**
- Based on the true story of Hadji Murad, a legendary Avar warrior
- Tolstoy served in the Caucasus 1851-1854 and took part in campaigns against Chechen-Dagestani tribes
- Used material from Russian archives, including Hadji Murad's own account of his life
- Tolstoy admitted he had to "write it on the sly from myself" since it betrayed his didactic aims for art

**Critical acclaim:**
- Harold Bloom called it "my personal touchstone for the sublime of prose fiction, to me the best story in the world"

### Confession (1882)
Autobiographical account of Tolstoy's spiritual crisis, written 1879-1880.

**Key elements:**
- Documents his existential crisis and contemplation of suicide
- The central question: "If God does not exist, since death is inevitable, what is the meaning of life?"
- Describes his search for meaning through philosophy, science, and finally faith
- Tolstoy stated: "I felt there was nothing beneath my feet anymore... And I no longer had any prop to help me live"

### The Kingdom of God Is Within You (1894)
Christian anarchist philosophical treatise, first published in Germany after being banned in Russia.

**Significance:**
- Culmination of 30 years of Tolstoy's thinking on nonviolence
- Lays out organization for society based on universal love
- Key text for Tolstoyan nonviolence and Christian anarchism
- Gandhi wrote it "overwhelmed" him and "left an abiding impression"
- Gandhi listed it among the three most important modern influences in his life

### What Is Art? (1897)
Philosophical treatise on aesthetics.

**Central theory:**
- Rejects notions of aesthetics centered around beauty
- Art is defined by its role in transmitting feelings between human beings
- Art's purpose is promoting mutual understanding and human unity
- Firmly denies that pleasure is art's sole purpose

**Controversial judgments:**
- Dismissed Beethoven, Wagner, Shakespeare, and Dante
- Labeled his own major works as "bad art"
- Excepted only "God Sees the Truth" and "Prisoner of the Caucasus" from his own corpus

### Other Notable Works

- **Childhood** (1852) - First novel, fictitious account of his youth
- **The Cossacks** (1863) - Early novella about Caucasus life
- **Sevastopol Sketches** (1855) - First brought literary recognition
- **What I Believe** (1884) - Religious philosophy
- **The Kreutzer Sonata** (1889) - Controversial novella advocating sexual abstinence

## Historical Context

### Early Life (1828-1851)
- Born to Count Nikolai Ilyich Tolstoy (1794-1837), a veteran of the Patriotic War of 1812
- Mother: Princess Mariya Tolstaya (nee Volkonskaya; 1790-1830)
- Fourth of five children
- Mother died before he was two years old
- Father died when he was nine (1837)
- After two guardians died, lived with aunt in Kazan
- Cousin Tatyana Aleksandrovna Yergolskaya had greatest influence on his childhood
- Educated at home by tutors
- Enrolled at University of Kazan in 1844 as student of Oriental languages
- Left university in 1847 without degree
- Returned to Yasnaya Polyana to manage estate and improve lot of serfs
- Early years marked by gambling, drinking, and dissipation
- Lost his religious faith at age 18

### Military Service (1851-1856)
- Joined older brother Nikolay in the Caucasus in 1851
- Served with artillery brigade
- Took part in campaigns against Chechen tribes and Imam Shamil
- Participated in Crimean War at Sevastopol (1853-56)
- Began literary career during military service
- *Sevastopol Sketches* (1855) brought first recognition
- Direct experience of war shaped his later pacifism

### Early Literary Success (1856-1869)
- Traveled Europe twice (1857, 1860-1861)
- Founded 13 schools for peasant children at Yasnaya Polyana after emancipation of serfs (1861)
- Described school principles in essay "The School at Yasnaya Polyana" (1862)
- Married Sophia Behrs on September 23, 1862 (she was 18, he was 34)
- Wrote *War and Peace* between 1862-1869

### The Marriage to Sophia Behrs
**The beginning:**
- On the eve of wedding, Tolstoy gave Sophia his diaries detailing sexual relations with female servants
- Diaries included fact that he had fathered a child by a woman on the estate
- Sophia was "devastated and disgusted"

**Sophia's contributions:**
- Copied and edited *War and Peace* manuscript seven times by candlelight
- Translated, copied, and edited his manuscripts
- Delivered documents, managed estates, handled outside affairs
- Served as business manager while bearing 13 children

**Sources of conflict:**
- Sophia struggled with Tolstoy's increasing devotion to spiritual matters
- Disputes over his desire to give away all private property
- Sophia was impatient with his ideas about social reform and simple living
- Over time, Tolstoy stopped trusting her and began hiding manuscripts

**The Kreutzer Sonata controversy (1889):**
- The novel's narrator murders his wife in jealousy
- Assumed to describe the Tolstoys' unhappy marriage
- Sophia wrote two novellas in response: "Whose Fault?" (1891-94) and "Song Without Words" (1898), unpublished until 2000

### Major Works Period (1869-1877)
- *Anna Karenina* serialized 1873-1877
- Growing family (13 children total)
- Deepening interest in education and social reform

### Spiritual Crisis and Conversion (1877-1880)
**The crisis:**
- 1877-1878: Complete change in outlook, described by son Sergei
- Biographer Aylmer Maude refers to his "fierce five-year inner struggle with doubt"
- At age fifty (1878), suffered most intense bout of self-doubt - became severely depressed, even suicidal
- Documented in *Confession* (written 1879-1880)

**Before conversion:**
- In *What I Believe*, Tolstoy wrote: "I am fifty-five years old and, with the exception of the fourteen or fifteen years of my childhood, I have been until recently a 'Nihilist'... I have not been a Socialist or Revolutionist, but a Nihilist in the sense of being completely without faith"

**The conversion:**
- Tolstoy stated that as soon as he said "God is Life," life was suffused with meaning
- Adopted literal interpretation of Jesus's teachings, particularly Sermon on the Mount
- "Commandment of non-resistance to evil by force"
- Became fervent Christian anarchist and anarcho-pacifist

**Religious views after conversion:**
- Rejected the sacraments, the priesthood, and the divinity of Jesus
- Believed Russian Orthodox Church had distorted Christ's message into a tool of state power
- Saw true essence of Christianity as love, humility, and nonviolence

### Excommunication (1901)
**The Decree:**
- Published by Holy Synod of Russian Orthodox Church on February 24, 1901
- Charged: "A writer well known to the world, Russian by birth, Orthodox by baptism and education, Count Tolstoy has insolently risen against the Lord and His Christ"
- Accused of rejecting "the personal living God," denying "the Lord Jesus Christ - God-Man, Redeemer and Saviour," denying immaculate conception, not recognizing the afterlife

**Technical details:**
- Not a formal excommunication in strictest sense
- Synod said Tolstoy had "fallen away" from the Church
- Holy Synod cited a fact that had already taken place - Tolstoy had excommunicated himself

**Tolstoy's response:**
- Welcomed the excommunication as proof he had stayed true to the gospel
- Complained the decision was illegal under church rules
- Noted it incited "anger and hatred toward him, which went so far as threats of murder"

**Public reaction:**
- The public adored Tolstoy and "laughed at the Synod's Decree"
- At March 1901 art exhibition, Tolstoy's portrait by Ilya Repin was showered with flowers

**Modern aftermath:**
- In the 2000s, requests to reconsider the 1901 decision were sent to the Church
- Because Tolstoy didn't publicly repent, the Church responded negatively each time

### Late Period (1880-1910)
- Renounced copyright on post-1881 works
- Gave away property (major conflict with wife and family)
- Became international symbol of moral authority
- Corresponded with Gandhi (1909-1910)
- Received Nobel Prize nominations: Literature (1902-1906), Peace (1901, 1902, 1909) - never awarded (remains a major Nobel Prize controversy)

### Final Flight and Death (November 1910)
**The departure:**
- Night of October 28, 1910: Tolstoy secretly left home
- Accompanied by Dr. Makovetsky, dressed in peasant garb, boarded third-class train
- Left letter to wife: his life was a burden, he required solitude, requested no attempts to find him
- Left after overhearing Sophia searching his study for his will

**The journey:**
- Became national sensation
- Located two days later at a monastery, then gone again
- Arrived at Astapovo railway station, 250 miles southeast of Moscow

**Death:**
- November 15: Fell ill with pneumonia
- Taken into the stationmaster's house
- Sophia rushed to his side but was not allowed to see him until final moments
- Died November 20, 1910 (age 82) of heart failure
- Representatives of press converged, turning his death into Russia's first great mass media event
- Station renamed "Lev Tolstoy" in 1918
- House became branch of Lev Tolstoy State Museum in 1939

## Tolstoy and Gandhi: A Transformative Correspondence

### The Impact of *The Kingdom of God Is Within You*
- Gandhi wrote in autobiography *The Story of My Experiments with Truth* that the book "overwhelmed" him and "left an abiding impression"
- Listed it among three most important modern influences (with Ruskin's *Unto This Last* and poet Shrimad Rajchandra)
- Gandhi in 1928: "At that time, I was skeptical about many things... I was a votary of violence. I had faith in it and none in nonviolence. After I read this book, lack of faith in nonviolence vanished"

### The Correspondence (1909-1910)
- Began with Tolstoy's "A Letter to a Hindu" (December 14, 1908) to Tarak Nath Das
- Letter argued only through love could India gain independence from colonial rule
- Gandhi wrote to request permission to reprint in his South African newspaper *Indian Opinion* (1909)
- Gandhi translated the letter into Gujarati
- Exchange evolved into year-long correspondence until Tolstoy's death

### Tolstoy Farm
- 1910: Gandhi and Hermann Kallenbach established commune in South Africa named "Tolstoy Farm"
- Families lived simply, worked collectively, practiced nonviolence, truth, and self-reliance
- Became training ground for Gandhi's movement

### Legacy Chain
- Tolstoy influenced Gandhi's concept of satyagraha (truth-force)
- Gandhi influenced Martin Luther King Jr., who traveled to India in 1959
- King called Gandhi "the guiding light of our technique of nonviolent social change"
- The genealogy: Tolstoy -> Gandhi -> King

## Philosophical Foundations

### The Moral Question
For Tolstoy, all questions ultimately reduce to: How should we live? Philosophy, art, religion - all must answer this or they are worthless.

### Authenticity vs. Convention
Most people live by convention - doing what is expected, pursuing what society values - rather than asking what is truly good. This false life leads to spiritual death while still physically alive. Ivan Ilyich is the paradigmatic example.

### The Wisdom of the Simple
Peasants and simple people often possess intuitive wisdom that educated people have lost. Sophistication can be a form of elaborate self-deception. Gerasim in *The Death of Ivan Ilyich* represents authentic life.

### Christian Anarchism
- Rejection of state authority and violence
- Literal interpretation of Jesus's teachings
- "Do not resist evil by force" (Sermon on the Mount)
- The state, the church, the military, the courts - all institutions of violence
- Influenced Gandhi's satyagraha and Martin Luther King Jr.'s nonviolent resistance

### The Doctrine of Non-Resistance
Evil cannot be overcome by evil. Violence perpetuates itself. Only by refusing to participate in violence - even defensive violence - can the cycle be broken.

### Philosophy of History (*War and Peace*)
- History is not created by great men
- Events result from millions of individual chains of cause and effect too small to be analyzed
- Even emperors are caught in chains of circumstance, though they imagine they rule the world
- Critiques theories that give power to individual "great men"

### On Death
- Most people live in denial of death
- Only by facing mortality can we understand life
- Death strips away pretense and reveals what matters
- Preparation for death begins with proper attitude toward life

## Literary Techniques

### Defamiliarization (Ostranenie)
Viktor Shklovsky, Russian Formalist critic, coined this term in 1917 using Tolstoy as the primary example.

**Definition:** The artistic technique of forcing the audience to see common things in an unfamiliar or strange way, to enhance perception of the familiar.

**Tolstoy's use:**
- In *War and Peace*, Natasha at the opera sees "painted cardboard and oddly dressed men and women who moved, spoke and sang strangely in a patch of blazing light"
- In "Kholstomer," the narrator is a horse, making property and ownership seem absurd
- Shklovsky noted Tolstoy's "several hundred examples... [applied] the device of estrangement to his [descriptions] of the dogmas and rituals" of Orthodox Church and Russian life

**Purpose:** Tolstoy used defamiliarization for social criticism - forcing readers to reassess "the habitualized normalities of the Russian elite"

**Influence:** Connected to Brecht's Verfremdungseffekt ("estrangement effect"); became central concept of twentieth-century art

### The Panoramic to Particular Method
Tolstoy moves seamlessly between the vast (historical forces, battlefields, social movements) and the intimate (a character's thought, a physical gesture).

### Free Indirect Discourse
Entering a character's consciousness while maintaining narrative distance, allowing the reader to see both the character's perspective and its limitations.

### Physical Observation as Psychology
Inner states revealed through precise observation of bodies: gestures, postures, facial expressions, ways of walking and sitting.

### Multiple Perspectives
The same event shown from different viewpoints, each partial and limited, together approaching a larger truth.

## Influence and Legacy

### On Literature
- Dostoevsky: Thought Tolstoy the greatest of living novelists
- Gustave Flaubert: Admired Tolstoy's work
- Virginia Woolf: Called Tolstoy "the greatest of all novelists"
- Thomas Mann: Compared *War and Peace* to Homer's *Iliad*
- James Wood: "the most luminous and capacious" of 19th-century novelists
- Gary Saul Morson: Called *War and Peace* "the greatest of all novels"
- Harold Bloom: Called *Hadji Murad* "the best story in the world"

### Tolstoy vs. Dostoevsky
- George Steiner's *Tolstoy or Dostoevsky*: Positions Tolstoy as "the foremost heir to the tradition of the epic" and Dostoevsky as "one of the major dramatic tempers after Shakespeare"
- Dmitry Merezhkovsky (*L. Tolstoy and Dostoyevsky*): Tolstoy as successor of "the earthly principal, the Human Truth" vs. Dostoevsky as bearer of "the spiritual principle, the Divine Truth"
- Merezhkovsky: "He and Dostoevsky are both akin and opposite to each other, just as the most powerful branches of a tree are; diverging in opposite directions at the top, while fused into one trunk at their base"

### On Philosophy
- Wittgenstein: Called Tolstoy "a true genius"
- Influenced existentialist themes of authentic living
- William James: Praised Tolstoy's psychological insight
- Anticipated phenomenological approaches to consciousness

### On Social Movements
- Gandhi: Credited *The Kingdom of God Is Within You* with transforming his worldview
- Martin Luther King Jr.: Influenced through Gandhi
- Tolstoyan communities established in multiple countries

### Nobel Prize Controversy
- Received nominations for Nobel Prize in Literature every year from 1902 to 1906
- Nominated for Nobel Peace Prize in 1901, 1902, and 1909
- Never awarded - remains a major Nobel Prize controversy

## Integration Notes

### Complementary Experts
- **Dostoevsky** - The great counterpoint: psychological depth, urban consciousness, religious questioning (though from different conclusions)
- **Gandhi** - Direct disciple of Tolstoy's nonviolent philosophy
- **Thoreau** - Shared interest in simple living and civil disobedience
- **Chekhov** - Acknowledged Tolstoy's mastery while developing his own subtle style
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** - Inheritor of Tolstoy's nonviolent legacy through Gandhi

### When to Use Tolstoy
- Exploring moral questions about how to live
- Examining authenticity versus convention
- Analyzing systems and institutions critically
- Rendering large-scale contexts (historical, social) alongside individual experience
- Questioning received wisdom and assumptions
- Grounding abstract ideas in concrete physical detail
- Applying defamiliarization to make the familiar strange
- Exploring the relationship between individual lives and historical forces

### Tone Guidance
- Morally serious but not preachy
- Observant of physical detail
- Simultaneously panoramic and intimate
- Questioning of convention
- Compassionate but unflinching
- Simple language for complex truths
- The narrator's moral awareness present but not didactic
